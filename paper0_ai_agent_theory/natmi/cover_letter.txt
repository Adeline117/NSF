WHY NATURE MACHINE INTELLIGENCE

This work sits at the intersection of information theory, machine
learning, and AI governance -- precisely the interdisciplinary space that
Nature Machine Intelligence serves. Our result speaks simultaneously to
the ML community (a new bound on classifier performance), the policy
community (quantitative limits on behavioral regulation), and the broader
AI-safety audience (whether agentic AI systems can be reliably detected
from behavior alone). The broad readership of NatMI is the ideal venue
for a finding with implications across all three of these communities.

CORE CONTRIBUTION

We provide the first information-theoretic bound on the accuracy of
behavioral AI agent detection. Using Fano's inequality, we prove that
any classifier mapping behavioral features to agent categories obeys an
accuracy ceiling determined by the mutual information between features
and labels. Applied to 2,744 real agent deployments on public
blockchains -- the only setting where both complete behavioral records
and provenance-verified ground-truth labels are available -- we show
that the ceiling is 97.2% and that existing classifiers already reach
95.3%. The gap of roughly two percentage points means that behavioral
detection is essentially saturated: further classifier engineering
cannot close it.

KEY DISTINCTION FROM PRIOR WORK

Prior detection work (social-media bots, frontrunning agents,
AI-generated text) has been purely empirical: a new classifier is
proposed, accuracy is reported, and the tacit assumption is that
accuracy can always be improved with more data or better models. Our
contribution is to prove that this assumption is false by establishing a
fundamental limit. The bound is a property of the observation channel,
not of any specific classifier, dataset, or domain. This shifts the
research programme from "build a better classifier" to "open a new
observation channel" -- a qualitative reorientation with direct policy
consequences.

BROADER RELEVANCE

Although we measure the bound on blockchain transaction data -- chosen
because it is the only domain where I(X;Y) can be directly estimated
from public records -- the result generalizes to any behavioral
detection task. We show that the same Fano-based protocol (estimate
mutual information, invert the inequality, report headroom) applies
wherever a labeled behavioral corpus exists. The policy implication is
immediate: the EU AI Act, U.S. Executive Order 14110, and the UK AI
regulation framework all presuppose that AI agents can be behaviorally
identified at fine category granularity. Our analysis shows this is
structurally bounded, and that durable governance requires provenance
registration or execution-layer attestation rather than behavioral
observation alone.
