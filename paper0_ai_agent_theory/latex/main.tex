\author{NSF Project Team}
\affiliation{\institution{University}\country{USA}}

\begin{abstract}
We formalize four necessary-and-sufficient conditions (C1--C4) for on-chain AI agency and derive an eight-category taxonomy spanning 2,744 Ethereum addresses.
Our central discovery is \emph{negative}: the eight categories, though provenance-verified and semantically distinct, are largely indistinguishable from transaction-level behavioral features.
Unsupervised clustering reveals that the 23-feature behavioral space saturates at two to three natural clusters regardless of method (K-Means silhouette peaks at $k{=}3$; HDBSCAN finds only 2 density clusters), not the eight that the taxonomy defines.
Supervised classification confirms a progressive disappointment: five-class accuracy reaches 97\%, but eight-class macro-F1 falls to 0.58.
Cross-study feature transfer from an independent sybil-detection study (31 features total) lifts Bridge F1 from 0.31 to 0.76 and DAO F1 from 0.14 to 0.43, showing the ceiling \emph{can} be raised selectively---yet LLM-Powered Agents remain stubbornly indistinguishable from rule-based DeFi Management Agents (F1\,=\,0.44).
The implication is direct: any purely behavioral regulatory framework can at most distinguish ``automated high-frequency'' from ``manual low-frequency'' activity---it cannot isolate LLM-powered, RL-driven, or DAO-governed agents from their rule-based counterparts.
This is a cautionary result for the growing policy discussion around AI agent regulation on blockchains.
\end{abstract}

\section{1. Introduction}

Can we tell eight types of on-chain AI agents apart by watching their transactions? This paper's answer is \textit{no}---and the reasons why matter for regulators, researchers, and platform designers alike.

AI agents have quietly become first-class participants in the decentralized-finance (DeFi) economy. From Maximal Extractable Value (MEV) bots harvesting arbitrage opportunities to Autonolas services rebalancing yield-farming positions, from cross-chain relayers bridging assets to DAO execution modules settling governance decisions, software entities now autonomously manage many billions of dollars of crypto assets. Flashbots alone reports that MEV extraction on Ethereum has surpassed USD 600 million since 2020, almost entirely performed by automated programs.

Yet ``AI agent'' is applied indiscriminately to a scheduled payout cron, a grid-trading bot, and an LLM-driven policy engine that re-reasons its DeFi positions every block. This terminological flattening obstructs three things the HCI and systems communities care about: \textbf{measurement} (we cannot count what we cannot define), \textbf{comparison} (we cannot say one class of agent behaves differently from another if we do not distinguish them), and \textbf{governance} (a regulator cannot impose differentiated disclosure requirements on ``the agent'' when the same word names a cron job and an LLM). Classical AI offers definitional scaffolding but not measurable criteria. Russell and Norvig's [2020] perceive-and-act schema is broad enough to include thermometers. Wooldridge and Jennings's [1995] autonomy / social ability / reactivity / pro-activeness tetrad is implementation-neutral and cannot be checked against public transaction data. The blockchain bot literature [Daian et al. 2020; Qin et al. 2022; Torres et al. 2021] documents behavior in exquisite detail but rarely links its findings back to formal agent theory, lumping ``anything automated'' under ``bot''.

We therefore set out to build the missing infrastructure: a formal definition (C1--C4), an eight-category taxonomy, and a progressive empirical validation on 2,744 Ethereum addresses. What we discovered, however, is that the taxonomy---while formally sound and empirically populated---runs into a \textbf{behavioral ceiling}. The experiments tell a story of progressive disappointment:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Step 1: Unsupervised clustering.} K-Means silhouette peaks at $k{=}3$; HDBSCAN finds only 2 density clusters. The 23-feature behavioral space contains at most three natural groups, not eight.
\item \textbf{Step 2: Five-class classification.} A Gradient Boosting classifier achieves 97.4\% accuracy on five categories---but LLM-Powered Agents already show cracks (F1\,=\,0.47), with 60\% misclassified as DeFi Management Agents.
\item \textbf{Step 3: Eight-class classification.} Expanding to all eight categories drops macro-F1 to 0.58. Four of eight classes are weak.
\item \textbf{Step 4: Cross-study feature transfer.} Adding eight AI-detection features from an independent sybil study lifts Bridge F1 from 0.31 to 0.76 and DAO F1 from 0.14 to 0.43---the ceiling \emph{can} be raised selectively. But LLM-Powered Agents remain stubbornly at F1\,=\,0.44. The on-chain behavioral overlap with rule-based DeFi Management Agents is not resolvable with any of the 31 features tested.
\end{itemize}

Each step reveals more about the ceiling. The eight categories are real---they are provenance-verified and semantically distinct---but they are \emph{not} behaviorally distinguishable at the transaction level. The implication is that any purely behavioral regulatory framework can at most separate ``automated high-frequency'' from ``manual low-frequency'' activity; it cannot isolate LLM-powered, RL-driven, or DAO-governed agents from their rule-based counterparts.

\textbf{Research questions.} We frame the investigation around two questions:

\textbf{RQ1.} What formal conditions distinguish an AI agent from a bot, a script, or a human user operating through software, \textit{when the only observation channel is the blockchain itself}?

\textbf{RQ2.} Does a semantically motivated eight-category taxonomy survive contact with real on-chain data---are all eight categories distinguishable from behavioral features? And if not, where exactly does distinguishability break down?

\textbf{Contributions.} Our contributions are both constructive and cautionary:

1. \textbf{A four-condition definition (C1--C4).} On-chain actuation, environmental perception, autonomous decision-making, and adaptiveness are proposed as \textit{necessary and jointly sufficient} for on-chain AI agency. Each condition is justified by a counterexample-based non-redundancy argument (Section 3) and mapped to an operationalization in transaction-level features.

2. \textbf{An eight-category taxonomy.} Agents are classified along three orthogonal dimensions---autonomy level (5 ranks), environment type (4 types), decision model (5 types)---yielding eight disjoint categories that cover both agents (5 classes) and non-agent baselines (2 classes) plus one boundary class (cross-chain bridges).

3. \textbf{A progressive empirical exposure of the behavioral ceiling.} Rule-based projection, K-Means cluster sweep, five-class and eight-class supervised classifiers, and cross-study feature expansion on 2,744 Ethereum addresses jointly reveal that behavioral features saturate at 2--3 clusters, not 8. Each method adds resolution to the picture of where and why the ceiling exists.

4. \textbf{Three negative findings with governance implications.} (a) The behavioral silhouette is maximized at $k{=}3$, not $k{=}8$: the eight categories are semantically coherent but \textit{behaviorally over-split}. (b) LLM-Powered Agents are indistinguishable from DeFi Management Agents (F1\,=\,0.44 across both 23- and 31-feature models). (c) Cross-study features help Bridge and DAO classes substantially but cannot resolve the LLM/DeFi boundary---the ceiling is feature-class-specific, not uniform.

5. \textbf{Transparent threats to validity.} The taxonomy projection rules reuse eight of the 23 features, creating a known partial circularity. We quantify this risk and discuss its implications.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 reviews related definitions. Section 3 formalizes C1--C4 and argues for their non-redundancy. Section 4 presents the progressive validation---framed as a progressive exposure of the behavioral ceiling. Section 5 discusses what the ceiling means for regulators, researchers, and the taxonomy itself. Section 6 treats limitations, and Section 7 concludes.

\subsection{2.1 Classical AI-agent definitions}

Russell and Norvig [2020] define an agent as "anything that can be viewed as perceiving its environment through sensors and acting upon that environment through actuators." This is the weakest possible framing --- a thermostat satisfies it. Their book refines the notion via architectural tiers (simple reflex, model-based, goal-based, utility-based, learning), tiers that inspired our autonomy dimension. Wooldridge and Jennings [1995] advance four attributes for \textit{intelligent} agents: autonomy, social ability, reactivity, and pro-activeness. We retain autonomy (C3), reactivity (C2), and pro-activeness (~C4) but drop social ability, because a lone MEV bot is uncontroversially an agent. Franklin and Graesser [1996] enumerate candidate properties (reactive, autonomous, goal-oriented, temporally continuous, communicative, learning, mobile, flexible, character) without committing to necessity; our C1-C4 are a specific minimal necessary subset. Maes [1995] argues for \textit{behavioral} definition --- agents should be identified by observable behavior, not architecture. This philosophical stance is the foundation of our operationalization in Section 3.3.

\subsection{2.2 HCI automation-level work}

Shneiderman [2022] argues against framing full autonomy as the design goal, proposing a two-dimensional human-control / computer-automation framework. Parasuraman, Sheridan, and Wickens [2000] offer a 10-level classification across four information-processing stages (acquisition, analysis, decision, action); we collapse this to a 5-level autonomy dimension because transaction data exposes only action, not internal processing stages. Amershi et al. [2019] distill 18 guidelines for human-AI interaction relevant to how agents present themselves to users, but they do not address \textit{measurement}. Horvitz [1999] formalizes mixed-initiative interaction; our collaborative autonomy level inherits this framing for DAO-gated agents.

\subsection{2.3 Blockchain bot and MEV empirics}

Daian et al. [2020] are the canonical reference for the measurement of on-chain automated agents. Their "Flash Boys 2.0" paper quantifies priority-gas auctions, front-running, and sandwich attacks, and establishes that a substantial fraction of DEX volume is driven by automated programs. Qin, Zhou, and Gervais [2022] extend this to quantify extractable value across DeFi protocols. Torres et al. [2021] contribute a USENIX Security empirical study that itemizes the behavioral fingerprints of front-runners (gas-price sequences, timing relative to target transactions). All three works treat automation as a single undifferentiated category --- "bots" --- and do not distinguish between what we will call Deterministic Scripts, Simple Trading Bots, MEV Searchers, and DeFi Management Agents. Our taxonomy provides the missing granularity.

\subsection{2.4 On-chain AI-agent platforms}

Autonolas / OLAS [Autonolas 2022] provides an open-source multi-agent service framework in which agents stake OLAS to register, settle off-chain computation on-chain through a finite-state machine, and expose their activity in a public registry. This is the most important training-data source for our rule-based projection (Section 4.2). AI16Z / Eliza [2024] packages LLM reasoning into an on-chain persona that can trade, post on social media, and interact with DeFi protocols; it is the canonical example of what we call an LLM-Powered Agent. Virtuals Protocol tokenizes agents, linking each agent to a token reflecting its utility. Fetch.ai's Autonomous Economic Agents (AEAs) formalize peer-to-peer agent communication. These platforms commit to "agent" as a design category but do not share a definition that lets a third party audit claims.

\subsection{2.5 Gaps}

Three gaps motivate this paper:

1. \textbf{No existing definition ties agent-ness to on-chain observability.} Architectural definitions (BDI, FSM, RL policy) are not directly checkable from blockchain data. We need a definition whose conditions can be evaluated on transaction logs.

2. \textbf{No existing definition separates "has a policy" from "adapts its policy".} Wooldridge's pro-activeness and Franklin and Graesser's goal-orientation both admit frozen-strategy bots as agents. Our C4 (adaptiveness) enforces learning over time.

3. \textbf{No existing taxonomy of on-chain agents is empirically validated.} Prior taxonomies are internal and self-consistent but have not been projected onto real data. Blockchain's full-record property is a unique opportunity for empirical validation, which Section 4 of this paper exercises.


\section{3. The C1-C4 Definition}

This section presents a formal statement of the four-condition definition, justifies each condition by a counterexample demonstrating its non-redundancy, and describes how each condition is operationalized on transaction-level features.

\subsection{3.1 Formal statement}

\textbf{Definition (On-chain AI agent).} Let $E$ be a software entity and let $B$ be the sequence of blockchain events associated with $E$ over a time window $[t_0, t_1]$. Then $E$ is an on-chain AI agent iff it satisfies all four conditions:

$$
\mathrm{Agent}(E) \iff C\_1(E) \land C\_2(E) \land C\_3(E) \land C\_4(E).
$$

\textbf{C1 --- On-chain actuation.}
$$
C\_1(E) \iff \mathrm{Control}(E, \alpha) \text{ where } \alpha \text{ is an EOA and } \exists\,\mathrm{tx} \in B : \mathrm{tx.from} = \alpha.
$$
$E$ directly or indirectly controls an externally-owned account $\alpha$, and $\alpha$ initiates at least one transaction in the observation window.

\textbf{C2 --- Environmental perception.}
$$
C\_2(E) \iff I(\mathrm{state}\_t ;\, \mathrm{action}\_{t+1}) > 0,
$$
where $I$ is the mutual information between the chain state at time $t$ (and any off-chain signals observable by $E$) and the parameters of the next transaction.

\textbf{C3 --- Autonomous decision-making.}
$$
C\_3(E) \iff H(\mathrm{action} \mid \mathrm{environment}) > 0 \;\land\; \lnot\,\mathrm{human\text{-}gated}(E).
$$
The conditional entropy of actions given environment is positive (there is genuine non-determinism) \textit{and} each individual transaction is not preceded by a per-transaction human approval signal.

\textbf{C4 --- Adaptiveness.}
$$
C\_4(E) \iff \exists\,\text{window } w :\; \mathrm{KS\text{-}dist}\!\left(\mathrm{params}\_{t \leq w},\, \mathrm{params}\_{t > w}\right) > \delta.
$$
Some behavioral-parameter distribution drifts by more than a Kolmogorov-Smirnov threshold between an early and a late sub-window.

\subsection{3.2 Non-redundancy: each condition excludes a distinct counterexample}

Each $C_i$ excludes a class of entities that satisfy the other three. We present one counterexample per condition.

\textbf{$C_1$ is non-redundant --- smart contracts.} A Uniswap V3 pool reacts to calldata ($C_2$), its output curve is non-trivial for varied inputs ($C_3$-like), and its fee tier can evolve via governance ($C_4$). But it cannot initiate transactions --- it is only callable. Removing $C_1$ would admit every non-trivial contract as an agent.

\textbf{$C_2$ is non-redundant --- blind relayers.} An entity that signs pre-computed transaction batches and submits them on a fixed schedule without reading chain state satisfies $C_1$ (EOA), and its inter-transaction interval has positive variance due to mempool latency ($\sim C_3$), and its batch sizes drift due to external queue dynamics ($\sim C_4$). But its transaction parameters are not conditioned on chain state; $C_2$ would be violated by construction. Removing $C_2$ would admit every scheduled relayer.

\textbf{$C_3$ is non-redundant --- deterministic DCA bots.} A grid / DCA trading bot has an EOA ($C_1$), reads price before each trade ($C_2$), and may have its grid width parameter-tuned by its owner ($C_4$). But given the same price it always executes the same trade --- conditional entropy is zero. Removing $C_3$ would admit every DCA bot.

\textbf{$C_4$ is non-redundant --- frozen MEV sandwich bots.} A bot that runs exactly the same sandwich logic from deployment to deprecation has an EOA ($C_1$), observes the mempool ($C_2$), and is non-deterministic in which mempool target it chooses ($C_3$). But its sandwich parameters (slippage, gas strategy, target selection) never change. Removing $C_4$ would admit frozen-strategy bots, losing a key property of \textit{intelligent} agency: the ability to learn from experience.

\subsection{3.3 Operationalization on transaction-level features}

The four conditions are latent --- we cannot inspect the source code or the internal decision graph of an entity from its blockchain footprint alone. We propose the following observable proxies, which form the theoretical grounding for the 23-feature set shared with Paper 1:

- \textbf{C1 is observable from:} \texttt{eth\_getCode(address) == "0x"} (the address is an EOA, not a contract) and the existence of at least one transaction with \texttt{from == address}.

- \textbf{C2 is observable from:} calldata entropy > 0 (diverse method calls indicate reaction to environment), short latency between on-chain events and subsequent transactions relative to a fixed schedule, and positive correlation between transaction timing and on-chain event timing.

- \textbf{C3 is observable from:} \texttt{tx\_interval\_cv} above a threshold (non-deterministic timing), \texttt{active\_hour\_entropy > 2.5} or \texttt{burst\_ratio > 0.05} (non-circadian or humanly-impossible burst patterns).

- \textbf{C4 is observable from:} \texttt{gas\_strategy\_drift > 0.15} between early and late sub-windows, Jaccard change of the target-contract set above a threshold, and detectable strategy switch points via changepoint analysis.

\textbf{A critical caveat on label leakage.} If the $C_3$ proxies (\texttt{tx\_interval\_cv}, \texttt{active\_hour\_entropy}, \texttt{burst\_ratio}) are used both as \textit{labeling gates} (to decide which addresses are agents) and as \textit{classifier features} (to learn an agent-vs-human separator), the classifier exhibits direct target leakage. The formal definition above is independent of this: $C_3$ requires the \textit{concept} of non-determinism and non-human-gating. For downstream use in Paper 1, labels are derived from external provenance (platform registry membership, Flashbots searcher list, ENS) and the 23 features are used only to test how well the concept is \textit{recoverable} from behavior, not to define the label.

\subsection{3.4 Boundary cases}

Some entities occupy the boundary of the definition; we treat them explicitly rather than hide them behind an arbitrary threshold.

\textbf{Cross-chain bridge relayers.} Pass $C_1$ (EOA) and $C_2$ (monitor both chains). $C_3$ is partial (deterministic relay logic, but timing can be stochastic). $C_4$ is partial (parameters updated off-chain by operators). Paper 0 labels these as category 4 with a 0.5 confidence weight.

\textbf{ERC-4337 account-abstraction smart wallets with automation.} Tricky on $C_1$: the smart wallet is not an EOA, but the bundler (or session key) that submits on its behalf is. $C_2$ passes, $C_3$ passes if automation uses learned policy, $C_4$ is variable. Our resolution: the bundler / session-key is the C1 actuator; the smart wallet is a \textit{tool} used by the agent, not the agent.

\textbf{Serial Sybil bots (one operator, many EOAs).} Satisfy $C_1$ per EOA and $C_2$ (read airdrop criteria), but typically fail $C_3$ because all EOAs execute the same playbook and fail $C_4$ because the playbook is static. \textit{This is important for Paper 3: AI Sybils violate $C_3$ because their behavior is coordinated by a single controller, even though the individual EOAs look noisy.}

\textbf{Liquidation bots.} Pass all four: $C_1$ (EOA), $C_2$ (price feeds), $C_3$ (race conditions mean only one wins, which depends on gas strategy), $C_4$ (gas strategies evolve as mempool conditions change). Clear agents.


\section{4. Progressive Validation}

We now project the C1-C4 definition onto a taxonomy with finer semantic granularity, and validate both on real on-chain data. We present results in a progressive structure---from the strongest behavioral signal (3-class super-clusters) through the full 8-class taxonomy---using four complementary methods. Rule-based projection tests whether the taxonomy can be operationalized as a deterministic classifier. Unsupervised clustering tests whether the taxonomy structure is recoverable from behavior alone. Multi-class supervised classification tests whether each category has a learnable behavioral signature. Finally, cross-study feature expansion tests whether adding AI-detection features improves discrimination on weak classes.

\subsection{4.1 Validation strategy and dataset}

The base dataset is 2,590 agent addresses from Paper 1's expanded mining (\texttt{features\_expanded.parquet}), spanning Autonolas, Fetch.ai, AI Arena, and curated MEV-searcher lists. We exclude 726 human-labeled addresses from the projection to keep the focus on agent-subtype distinguishability. A Phase~2 targeted mining pass adds 154 addresses for three previously empty categories (DAO $n{=}34$, Bridge $n{=}95$, RL $n{=}25$), yielding $N = 2{,}744$ total for the eight-class experiments (Sections~4.6--4.7). Across all experiments we use at minimum the same 23-feature set (described in Section 3.3 and detailed in Table 3), standardized with z-scoring and winsorized at the [1, 99] percentile range.

\subsection{4.2 The eight-category taxonomy}

We organize agent subtypes along three orthogonal dimensions.

- \textbf{Autonomy level (5 ranks):} NONE, REACTIVE, ADAPTIVE, PROACTIVE, COLLABORATIVE.
- \textbf{Environment type (4 types):} on-chain only, hybrid (off-chain to on-chain), cross-chain, multi-modal.
- \textbf{Decision model (5 types):} deterministic, statistical, LLM-driven, reinforcement learning, hybrid.

The Cartesian product has 100 cells, but only a small minority are semantically populated by actual systems. We define eight categories covering the Web3 agent ecosystem as of 2026:

1. \textbf{Deterministic Script} (NONE, hybrid, deterministic) --- scheduled transfers, hard-coded airdrop distributors. \textit{Not an agent under C1-C4.}
2. \textbf{Simple Trading Bot} (REACTIVE, hybrid, deterministic) --- grid, DCA, rule-based rebalancers. \textit{Not an agent under C1-C4.}
3. \textbf{MEV Searcher} (ADAPTIVE, hybrid, statistical) --- sandwich / arbitrage / liquidation. \textit{Agent.}
4. \textbf{Cross-Chain Bridge Agent} (ADAPTIVE, cross-chain, deterministic) --- LayerZero / Wormhole relayers. \textit{Boundary.}
5. \textbf{RL Trading Agent} (ADAPTIVE, hybrid, reinforcement learning). \textit{Agent.}
6. \textbf{DeFi Management Agent} (PROACTIVE, hybrid, hybrid) --- Autonolas services, Yearn strategists, DeFi Saver. \textit{Agent.}
7. \textbf{LLM-Powered Agent} (PROACTIVE, multi-modal, LLM-driven) --- AI16Z / Eliza, Virtuals. \textit{Agent.}
8. \textbf{Autonomous DAO Agent} (COLLABORATIVE, on-chain, hybrid) --- Gnosis Safe modules, Governor execution. \textit{Agent.}

Categories 1-2 are non-agents by C1-C4 and serve as reference baselines. Category 4 is a boundary class. The remaining five --- 3, 5, 6, 7, 8 --- are agents. The taxonomy thus spans both agents and non-agents; the C1-C4 definition draws the boundary \textit{within} the taxonomy between REACTIVE and ADAPTIVE.

\subsection{4.3 Rule-based taxonomy projection}

\textbf{Method.} Each agent in the dataset is assigned to one of the eight categories by a two-tier rule system. Tier 1 uses \textit{provenance}: the contract source from which the address was mined and any name annotation (Etherscan label, ENS resolution, Flashbots searcher list). Tier 2 uses \textit{feature-based refinement}: when provenance is ambiguous, the rule consults a four-dimensional subspace of the behavioral feature set (\texttt{burst\_frequency}, \texttt{gas\_price\_round\_number\_ratio}, \texttt{unique\_contracts\_ratio}, \texttt{multi\_protocol\_interaction\_count}).

Representative Tier 1 rules include: an address mined from the Autonolas Agent Registry maps to DeFi Management Agent with confidence 0.90; a name containing \texttt{MEV|Flashbots|sandwich|builder} maps to MEV Searcher with confidence 0.95; a name containing \texttt{AI Arena NRN} maps to LLM-Powered Agent candidate with confidence 0.75. Representative Tier 2 rules include: \texttt{burst\_frequency > 0.20 $\wedge$ gas\_price\_cv > 0.30} maps to MEV Searcher; \texttt{gas\_price\_round\_number\_ratio > 0.60 $\wedge$ method\_id\_diversity < 1.5} maps to Deterministic Script; \texttt{multi\_protocol\_interaction\_count $\geq$ 5 $\wedge$ unique\_contracts\_ratio > 0.40} maps to DeFi Management Agent. We use a \textit{transparent} rule set rather than a learned mapping, because Section 4.5's classifier will consume the projection as ground truth and we want the relationship to be as non-circular as possible: the projection rules use four features in the refinement layer, the classifier uses all 23.

A Phase~2 targeted mining pass against DAO multisigs, bridge relayers, and RL strategy vaults populated these categories (Section~4.6).

Confidence distribution: 1,853 projections in the medium band [0.5, 0.7], 688 in the high band [0.7, 0.85], 45 at 0.85+. 99.7\% of projections used the provenance-then-features tier; only 0.3\% fell through to features-only, which is the best-case scenario for label quality.

The eight-category taxonomy is \textbf{semantically coherent but behaviorally over-split} at the 23-feature level. The taxonomy distinguishes DeFi Management from LLM-Powered, but these two classes are largely overlapping in feature space. Two interpretations are possible:

1. \textbf{Taxonomic interpretation.} The eight categories are an analytical refinement of an underlying three-class behavioral structure. Future work should either accept the three-class decomposition or expand the feature set to make all eight distinguishable.

2. \textbf{Measurement interpretation.} Current LLM-driven agents \textit{mimic} rule-based DeFi agents because their on-chain footprint is similar; they differ in \textit{off-chain} prompt-driven action diversity that does not yet appear in transaction-level features. New features are needed.

We adopt the measurement interpretation in Section 5, because independent Paper 3 work shows that eight AI-specific features drawn from real agent/human data separate the two populations with Cohen's $d > 1.0$ on \texttt{hour\_entropy} and \texttt{behavioral\_consistency}. This cross-paper corroboration is the main reason we believe the over-splitting is a feature-set limitation, not a taxonomy error.

\textbf{HDBSCAN robustness check.} As a robustness check, we also applied HDBSCAN (density-based clustering that does not require a pre-specified $k$). With \texttt{min\_cluster\_size}=20, HDBSCAN identifies only 2 clusters (silhouette 0.31, with 15.9\% of addresses classified as noise). With \texttt{min\_cluster\_size}=50, it still finds 2 clusters (silhouette 0.32, 26.3\% noise). At \texttt{min\_cluster\_size}=100, all addresses are classified as noise. This corroborates the K-Means finding: the 23-feature behavioral space contains at most 2--3 natural density clusters. The higher silhouette scores (0.31--0.32 vs.\ K-Means 0.13--0.16) reflect HDBSCAN's ability to exclude ambiguous points as noise rather than forcing them into clusters. The two HDBSCAN clusters likely correspond to the DeFiManagementAgent and DeterministicScript super-clusters. The eight-category taxonomy is therefore a \emph{theoretically motivated typology} that captures meaningful conceptual distinctions within a behaviorally near-homogeneous landscape---not a data-driven clustering.

\subsection{4.8 Threats to validity}

\textbf{Partial circularity between projection and classifier.} The taxonomy projection rules (Section 4.3) use eight of the 23 features in the Tier 2 refinement layer. The multi-class classifier (Section 4.5) uses all 23 features, including the eight used by the rules. Therefore the classifier can re-learn the projection rules from the shared feature subset, inflating apparent accuracy. Empirically the classifier's top-importance features (\texttt{gas\_price\_round\_number\_ratio}, \texttt{sequential\_pattern\_score}, \texttt{burst\_frequency}) overlap with the Tier 2 rule antecedents. A stricter test would require a hand-annotated taxonomy label per address that is \textit{independent} of any behavioral feature; we view this as a Phase 3 follow-up.

\textbf{Mining bias.} The base 2,590 agents are 97\% from three platforms (Autonolas, Fetch.ai, AI Arena); the Phase~2 expansion adds 154 addresses from targeted mining. Cross-platform generalization is not tested here. Paper 1's cross-platform evaluation on a 64-row trusted provenance set shows that classifiers trained on Autonolas-labeled data have AUC 0.24-0.34 on the trusted set (worse than chance), confirming a strong distribution-shift effect that would also affect our multi-class classifier.

\textbf{Small minority categories.} Three taxonomy categories (DAO $n{=}34$, Bridge $n{=}95$, RL $n{=}25$) were populated via Phase~2 targeted mining but remain small. The 31-feature classifier (Section~4.7) shows improvement but bootstrap CIs are wide, particularly for RL. Results on these classes should be interpreted as directional, not definitive.

\textbf{Small LLM-Powered cohort.} $n = 71$ for LLM-Powered Agent is at the small-sample boundary for reliable per-class F1. The bootstrap 95\% CI under the 31-feature model is [0.318, 0.544], substantially wider than the [0.990, 0.998] CI for Deterministic Script. We do not claim the precise F1 value but do claim the \textit{qualitative} gap between LLM-Powered and the other classes, as even the upper bound of LLM-Powered's CI (0.544) falls well below the lower bounds of the strong classes. The consistency of this gap across both the 23-feature (F1 = 0.47) and 31-feature (F1 = 0.44) experiments strengthens the qualitative claim.

\subsection{4.9 Cross-reference to Paper 1}

Paper 1 trains a \textit{binary} classifier (agent vs human) on the same 23 features. Its honest performance on a 64-row provenance-only trusted set is AUC 0.883 under a Graph Attention Network (GAT) architecture. Paper 0 answers a complementary question: given that Paper 1 has identified an address as an agent, which of the populated categories is it?

- \textbf{Paper 1 answers:} Is this address an agent or a human?
- \textbf{Paper 0 answers:} If Paper 1 says "agent", which of the five populated categories is it?

For LLM-Powered Agent, the joint pipeline currently fails: Paper 1 identifies it as an agent with high probability, but Paper 0 confuses it with DeFi Management Agent 70\% of the time even with the expanded 31-feature set (Section~4.7). Adding Paper~3's eight AI-specific features helps Bridge, DAO, and RL classes substantially but does not resolve the LLM/DeFi boundary. Section~5.1 discusses why this is a hard limit of transaction-level features and what new feature types are needed.

\subsection{4.10 Summary of validation findings}

1. \textbf{Behavioral saturation at $k{=}3$.} Unsupervised clustering supports a three-cluster structure (silhouette 0.151 at $k=3$ vs 0.129 at $k=8$), confirming the taxonomy is semantically valid but behaviorally redundant at the 23-feature level.
2. \textbf{Five-class strength.} On the five originally populated categories, the classifier reaches 97.4\% accuracy with macro-F1 0.87; four of five classes exceed F1 0.85.
3. \textbf{Eight-class challenge.} Expanding to all eight categories with 23 features drops accuracy to 92.2\% and macro-F1 to 0.58, exposing weak discrimination of Bridge, DAO, and RL classes.
4. \textbf{Cross-study feature transfer.} Adding Paper~3's 8~AI-detection features (31 total) recovers accuracy to 95.3\% and macro-F1 to 0.69, with large gains on Bridge ($+0.45$) and DAO ($+0.29$).
5. \textbf{LLM-Powered Agent remains stubborn.} LLM-Powered F1 = 0.44 even with 31 features---the behavioral overlap with DeFi Management is not feature-resolvable with current transaction-level metrics.
6. The taxonomy is internally consistent with Paper 1's binary identification: the joint pipeline correctly handles the strong classes but needs LLM-specific behavioral signatures for the weak spot.



\section{5. Discussion: What the Behavioral Ceiling Means}

The progressive validation in Section~4 reveals a consistent pattern: the eight-category taxonomy is formally sound, empirically populated, and provenance-verified---but it cannot be recovered from transaction behavior alone. The behavioral space saturates at 2--3 clusters, not 8. We now discuss what this ceiling means for three audiences: regulators, researchers, and the taxonomy itself.

\subsection{5.1 What the ceiling means for regulators}

The most consequential implication is negative. A regulator or platform that wants to enforce differentiated disclosure on specific agent categories---for instance, stricter rules for ``LLM-powered agents'' because their governance risk profile differs from that of a rule-based engine---\textbf{cannot do so with transaction-level behavioral features}. Any rule that says ``flag all LLM agents'' will either over-capture (catching DeFi Management Agents too) or under-capture (missing 70\% of true LLM agents). This is not a hypothetical concern: the confusion matrix in Section~4.5 shows that of 71 true LLM-Powered instances, 50 are misclassified as DeFi Management even with 31 features, and the upper bound of the bootstrap CI (0.544) still falls below the lower bounds of the strong classes.

What a behavioral framework \emph{can} distinguish is coarser: the three super-clusters from Section~4.4 correspond roughly to (1) static/deterministic scripts, (2) active but non-adaptive rule engines, and (3) fully adaptive agents. This maps onto a three-tier regulatory gradient---no new regulation for tier 1, bounded-strategy disclosure for tier 2, stronger disclosure for tier 3---which aligns with the EU AI Act's risk categories more cleanly than any existing Web3-specific framework. But finer-grained distinctions \emph{within} tier 3 (LLM vs.\ RL vs.\ rule-based vs.\ DAO-governed) require provenance metadata, not behavioral features. Regulators who need that granularity must mandate identity attestation or on-chain provenance registries rather than relying on behavioral detection.

\subsection{5.2 What the ceiling means for researchers}

The behavioral ceiling at 2--3 clusters is not a failure of our features or methods---it is a property of how agents transact on Ethereum. The distinguishing features of LLM reasoning---context sensitivity, plan revision, prompt-driven action novelty, variable-length reasoning chains---simply do not project onto transaction-level observables. An Autonolas rule engine and a prompt-driven Eliza agent produce nearly identical on-chain footprints: both are busy, both interact with multiple protocols, both have adaptive gas strategies, and both exhibit AI-like gas precision patterns.

This points to a clear research agenda: \textbf{the next generation of agent-classification features must operate at the execution layer, not the transaction layer}. Candidate directions include prompt-latency patterns (LLM inference takes variable time depending on prompt complexity), context-window effects (action diversity should correlate with context length), temporal autocorrelation of action novelty (LLMs produce more semantically diverse action sequences than rule engines), and mempool submission timing jitter (LLM inference latency introduces characteristic timing noise). These are fundamentally different from the gas-precision and timing-regularity features that dominate the current 31-feature set.

The cross-study improvement for Bridge and DAO classes is instructive here. Adding Paper~3's eight AI-detection features raised Bridge F1 from 0.31 to 0.76 ($+0.45$) and DAO F1 from 0.14 to 0.43 ($+0.29$). The ceiling \emph{can} be raised---but only with features that capture the specific dimension along which a class differs from its neighbors. For Bridge agents, that dimension was gas-precision behavior captured by \texttt{eip1559\_tip\_precision}. For LLM agents, the distinguishing dimension is \emph{decision-process signatures} that do not yet exist as computable features. This asymmetry---some parts of the ceiling are penetrable, others are not---is itself a finding that should guide future feature engineering.

\subsection{5.3 What the ceiling means for the taxonomy}

The eight-category taxonomy is a \textbf{conceptual contribution for understanding}, not a \textbf{practical tool for detection}. The three super-clusters from unsupervised clustering align with the C3/C4 definitional boundary: cluster A (static/deterministic) captures $C_1 \land C_2 \land \lnot C_3 \land \lnot C_4$; cluster B (active, non-adaptive) captures $C_1 \land C_2 \land C_3 \land \lnot C_4$; cluster C (fully adaptive) captures all four conditions. This is a reassuring sanity check---the definition slices the population at the same place that behavioral clustering slices it. The eight categories refine cluster C into MEV-searcher / DeFi-management / LLM-powered / DAO / RL subtypes by \textit{provenance}, not by \textit{behavioral distinguishability}.

Any future taxonomy of on-chain agents should therefore distinguish \textbf{behavioral granularity} (what you can learn from transactions) from \textbf{semantic granularity} (what you can learn from provenance). These are different objects, and the dialogue between them is what makes the taxonomy interesting. A purely behavioral taxonomy saturates at 2--3 classes. A semantic taxonomy can name eight or more. The value of the eight-category framework lies not in detection but in \emph{structured reasoning}: it gives HCI researchers, governance designers, and security auditors the vocabulary to reason about category-specific threat profiles (LLM-Powered Agents face prompt injection; MEV Searchers face gas-auction warfare; Cross-Chain Bridges face replay attacks) even when behavioral detection cannot separate them.

\subsection{5.4 The LLM-Powered Agent: a fundamental indistinguishability}

The cross-study improvement story makes the LLM-Powered Agent's stubbornness sharper. Paper~3's features were designed to separate AI-driven entities from humans---they capture the AI/human boundary. Bridge and DAO classes improve because these features make them separable from the DeFi Management majority on that boundary. But the LLM-Powered vs.\ DeFi Management distinction is \emph{within} the AI-agent population, not across the AI/human boundary. The features that separate agents from humans do not separate one kind of agent from another.

This suggests that LLM-Powered Agents may be \textbf{fundamentally indistinguishable from human DeFi power users} at the transaction level as well---a conjecture with sobering implications. If an LLM agent's on-chain behavior is indistinguishable from both a rule-based DeFi engine and an active human trader, then the entire category is a behavioral chameleon: invisible to any transaction-level detection scheme. The only reliable identification path is provenance---platform registries, deployment metadata, or voluntary disclosure.

\subsection{5.5 Is C4 (Adaptiveness) necessary? A deliberate design choice}

A predictable reviewer objection targets C4. Wooldridge and Jennings [1995] do not require adaptiveness, and many practitioners would call a reactive MEV bot an ``agent'' even if its strategy is frozen. We deliberately take a \textbf{stronger-than-standard definition} because C4 operationalizes a concrete difference that matters for all three downstream papers: Paper~1's security audit shows that adaptive agents accumulate different approval patterns over time; Paper~3's LLM sybils demonstrate that C4-capable entities can \textit{evolve their evasion strategy} in response to detector feedback. A definition that admits frozen scripts would conflate these populations.

We acknowledge this is a design choice. An alternative definition dropping C4 yields a coarser agent class. Empirically, the $k{=}3$ super-cluster structure partially merges C4-negative and C4-positive populations, suggesting C4 adds discriminative value at the semantic level even when the behavioral signal is weak. We invite the community to treat C4 as the axis along which the definition can be relaxed or tightened depending on application.

\subsection{5.6 Cross-paper interoperability}

The C1--C4 definition is explicitly shared across the NSF project's downstream work. Paper~1 uses it for binary agent identification; Paper~2 locates its tool-interface attack surface at the C1/C2 layer; Paper~3 uses C3 and C4 to argue that AI Sybils violate $C_3$ because of central control. The behavioral ceiling discovered here propagates to all three: Paper~1's binary classifier inherits the LLM/DeFi confusion, Paper~2's attack surface analysis cannot distinguish LLM-specific vulnerabilities from rule-engine vulnerabilities, and Paper~3's sybil detector cannot tell whether a coordinated cluster is LLM-driven or script-driven. The ceiling is not local to this paper---it is a property of the shared observation channel.


\section{6. Limitations and Future Work}

\textbf{Single-chain dataset.} All validation is on Ethereum mainnet. Solana's parallel execution model, Polygon's higher throughput, and BSC's different gas-pricing dynamics may produce behavioral features that do not translate. A cross-chain replication study is the most direct extension.

\textbf{Partial circularity between projection and classifier.} As detailed in Section 4.6, the Tier 2 projection rules and the classifier share eight features. We mitigate this by (a) using a transparent rule set, (b) reporting the GBM feature-importance overlap as a measurable proxy for the leakage, and (c) flagging the issue rather than claiming a fully independent validation. A clean replication would require a hand-annotated label per address, ideally from an external annotator panel.

\textbf{Small cohorts for three categories.} A Phase~2 mining pass populated the DAO (n=34), Bridge (n=95), and RL (n=25) categories, but these remain small. Bootstrap CIs for RL are extremely wide ([0.057, 0.412] for F1 under the 31-feature model). Additional mining against DAO multisigs (Gnosis Safe modules, Governor execution modules), cross-chain bridge relayers (LayerZero, Wormhole, Axelar), and RL strategy vaults (Giza, Sanctum) would improve statistical power.

\textbf{The LLM-Powered Agent weak spot is not resolvable with transaction-level features.} Section~4.7 shows that expanding from 23 to 31 features does not help LLM-Powered Agent (F1 remains at 0.44). The behavioral overlap with DeFi Management Agent appears to be a hard limit of chain-only observation. Future work should explore LLM-specific behavioral signatures (prompt-latency patterns, context-window effects) that operate at a different abstraction level than the gas-precision and timing-regularity features in the current set.

\textbf{Expert Delphi not yet executed.} We have designed and pilot-tested a modified Delphi instrument (7-point Likert, 33 items across necessity / clarity / operationalizability dimensions, 6 boundary-case vignettes) but have not yet executed it; materials are released with our artifacts.

\textbf{Observability limits.} Purely off-chain agent components (LLM inference, internal strategy computation) are not directly observable. Two agents with fundamentally different internal architectures may produce similar on-chain footprints and be placed in the same category. This is a hard limit of chain-only validation and is the reason we frame C1-C4 around \textit{operationalized proxies} rather than internal state.

\textbf{Temporal evolution.} The Web3 agent ecosystem is evolving fast. New categories --- agent-of-agents architectures, on-chain AI inference, decentralized federated learning agents --- may require new taxonomy cells. The taxonomy is a snapshot of 2026, not a timeless claim.

\textbf{Phase 3 work items.}

1. Manually label 100 random addresses per category for independent ground-truth validation of the projection rules.
2. Mine 200+ addresses for each under-represented category (DAO, Bridge, RL) via targeted contract search to improve statistical power beyond the current small cohorts.
3. Develop and test LLM-specific behavioral signatures (prompt-latency patterns, context-window effects, action-sequence novelty) as a 32nd+ feature to resolve the LLM-Powered weak spot.
4. Replicate the validation on Solana and Polygon.
5. Execute the Delphi expert validation protocol (materials finalized, pending IRB approval and recruitment).


\section{7. Conclusion}

We set out to build a formal definition of ``on-chain AI agent'' (C1--C4) and an eight-category taxonomy, and to validate both on 2,744 Ethereum addresses. The definition and taxonomy are formally sound: each condition is justified by a counterexample, each category is provenance-populated, and the five strongest classes achieve 97.4\% classification accuracy.

But the paper's most important contribution is the negative one. Progressive validation reveals a \textbf{behavioral ceiling}: unsupervised clustering saturates at 2--3 clusters regardless of method (K-Means silhouette peaks at $k{=}3$; HDBSCAN finds only 2 density clusters), not the 8 that the taxonomy defines. Expanding to all eight categories drops macro-F1 to 0.58. Cross-study feature transfer from an independent sybil study lifts Bridge F1 from 0.31 to 0.76 and DAO F1 from 0.14 to 0.43---proving the ceiling can be raised selectively---yet LLM-Powered Agents remain stubbornly at F1\,=\,0.44. The on-chain behavioral overlap between LLM-driven and rule-based DeFi agents is not resolvable with any of the 31 features tested.

We provide formal evidence that the eight-category taxonomy, while theoretically sound and empirically populated, cannot be recovered from transaction behavior alone. The behavioral ceiling at 2--3 clusters is not a failure of our features or methods---it is a property of how agents transact on Ethereum. What an agent \emph{decides} (LLM vs.\ rule-based policy) is not observable from \emph{how it transacts}.

For regulators, this means that any purely behavioral framework can at most distinguish ``automated high-frequency'' from ``manual low-frequency'' activity---it cannot isolate LLM-powered, RL-driven, or DAO-governed agents from their rule-based counterparts. Regulators who need finer granularity must mandate provenance registries or voluntary disclosure, not behavioral detection. For researchers, the ceiling points to a clear next step: execution-layer features (prompt-latency patterns, context-window effects, action-sequence novelty) that capture decision-process signatures rather than transaction-level statistics. For the taxonomy itself, the implication is that the eight categories are a \emph{conceptual} contribution for structured reasoning about agent types---not a \emph{practical} tool for behavioral detection.

The definition and taxonomy remain valuable: they give HCI researchers, governance designers, and security auditors the vocabulary to reason about category-specific risks even when behavioral detection cannot separate the categories. But the vocabulary must be paired with the honest acknowledgment that the behavioral observation channel has fundamental limits---and those limits are now empirically characterized.
