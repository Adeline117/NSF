\begin{abstract}
How good can feature-space sybil detection get against an adaptive adversary?
We evaluate LLM-driven sybil evasion across four detector modalities, \largeScaleNProjects{} Ethereum airdrop campaigns, and \largeScaleNAddresses{} addresses. Cross-modality evasion is near-total: \evasionThresholdRules{}00\% against threshold rules, \evasionLightgbmMl{}0\% against gradient-boosted ML, and \evasionGraphModerate{}--\evasionGraphAdvanced{}00\% against proxy graph detectors.
A \bestResponseNRounds{}-round best-response adversarial protocol between an LLM attacker (Claude Opus 4.6) and a retrained GBM defender converges to AUC $\bestResponseAucFloorMean{} \pm \bestResponseAucFloorStd{}$ across \bestResponseNSeeds{} seeds, establishing a \emph{practical detection floor} for feature-space approaches.
At convergence, the attacker's optimal blend ratio reaches \bestResponseEquilibriumBlendMean{}, meaning sybil profiles are 93\% identical to legitimate wallets---the residual 7\% of detectable signal concentrates in \texttt{behavioral\_consistency}, the single feature the attacker cannot fully suppress without abandoning coherent behavior (top-1 in \behavioralConsistencyTopInNRounds{}/\behavioralConsistencyTopTotalRounds{} seed-rounds).
At blend \bestResponseEquilibriumBlendMean{}, sybil profiles share 93\% of their distributional mass with legitimate wallets; that the classifier approaches chance under near-identical distributions is expected. The non-trivial finding is that \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} retains discriminative power even at this extreme blend, and that the convergence point is stable across seeds.
Closed-loop adversarial training with Claude Opus---where the LLM sees the detector's top features each round and generates targeted sybils---achieves Round-1 evasion of \closedLoopRoundLlmEvasionMean{}, exceeding the parametric attacker's \closedLoopRoundParametricEvasionMean{} by \closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$\times 100$ percentage points. This proves parametric approximations understate real-world LLM attacker capability.
We contribute a reusable leakage-audit checklist (validated by our own post-mortem: AUC \leakageFixLeakyAuc{}$\to$\leakageFixHonestAuc{} after fix) and argue that this ceiling result should redirect the sybil detection community from building better classifiers to building costlier execution environments, identity-layer defenses, and full-graph structural analysis.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{The Ceiling Question}

% FLUID: Opening claim depends on convergence result
How good can feature-space sybil detection get against an adaptive adversary? Our empirical answer is AUC $\approx \bestResponseAucFloorMean{}$---barely better than a coin flip. This number is not a performance metric to be improved; it is a \emph{practical detection floor} established by best-response convergence over \bestResponseNRounds{} rounds of adversarial training, and it represents the point at which \bestResponseNSeeds{} independent runs with different initial conditions converge to within \bestResponseAucFloorStd{} AUC of each other.

Airdrops are the default retroactive-reward mechanism for Ethereum-adjacent protocols, distributing hundreds of millions of dollars per campaign to users that satisfy eligibility heuristics. HasciDB~\cite{hascidb2026} is the most ambitious public audit of these distributions: using a modified-Delphi consensus of 12 Web3 practitioners it fixes five behavioral indicators---Burst Transactions (BT $\ge 5$), Burst Wallets (BW $\ge 10$), Hop Frequency (HF $\ge 0.80$), Repeat Funding (RF $\ge 0.50$) and Multi-Account (MA $\ge 5$)---and applies them to 3.6M eligible addresses across 16 Ethereum L1 campaigns (2020--2024), flagging roughly 30\% as Sybil.

All prior work shares a tacit assumption: \textit{the adversary runs a deterministic script}. The five indicators and the supervised models that learn them are fingerprints for classical batch-automation. They do not speak to an adversary that can \textit{read the rule} and \textit{plan against it}. Meanwhile, LLM-controlled on-chain agents have moved from curiosity to commodity. Our companion Paper~1~\cite{wen2026agent} labels 2,590 addresses as agents and 726 as humans and extracts 8 execution-layer features where all satisfy $p{<}0.01$ in Mann-Whitney U, with Cohen's $d$ up to 1.98 (\texttt{hour\_entropy}).

% FLUID: Convergence summary numbers
Rather than asking ``can LLMs evade?'' (they can---trivially), we ask the \emph{empirical} question: where does feature-space detection plateau under adaptive attack? We answer this by running a \bestResponseNRounds{}-round best-response protocol that converges across \bestResponseNSeeds{} seeds to AUC $\bestResponseAucFloorMean{} \pm \bestResponseAucFloorStd{}$. The stability of this convergence---and the persistence of \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} as the last informative feature---establishes a practical detection floor that motivates defense-in-depth.

\subsection{Attacker Model}

Throughout this paper the adversary is an LLM-powered wallet operator with:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Full read access} to HasciDB's 5 indicator definitions and thresholds (public in~\cite{hascidb2026}).
  \item \textbf{Access to empirical AI-feature distributions} from Paper~1 (also public).
  \item \textbf{Per-wallet planning} via \texttt{claude\_agent\_sdk} (Claude Opus 4.6), producing a JSON feature vector per wallet.
  \item \textbf{No live gas-token budget constraint} during evaluation; we measure evasion against feature-space detectors, not on-chain costs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research Questions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1 (Detection floor).} Does a multi-round best-response arms race between an LLM attacker and a retrained defender converge, and what is the convergence-point AUC---i.e., the empirical floor of feature-space detection?
  \item \textbf{RQ2 (Cross-modality evasion).} Do LLM-generated sybils evade all four detector modalities---rules, statistical ML, gradient-boosted ML, and graph-based community detection---simultaneously?
  \item \textbf{RQ3 (Last feature standing).} Which behavioral features survive adaptive attack, and why?
  \item \textbf{RQ4 (Baseline honesty).} What is the leakage-free performance of HasciDB detectors under cross-axis evaluation?
  \item \textbf{RQ5 (Beyond features).} What defense paradigms can break through the \bestResponseAucFloorMean{} ceiling?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Empirical detection floor.} A \bestResponseNRounds{}-round best-response adversarial protocol converging to AUC $\bestResponseAucFloorMean{} \pm \bestResponseAucFloorStd{}$ (\bestResponseNSeeds{} seeds, $\Delta\text{AUC}{<}0.01$ for the last 3 rounds), establishing a practical floor for feature-space sybil detection under adaptive LLM attack (Section~\ref{sec:equilibrium}).
  \item \textbf{Cross-modality evasion benchmark.} The first evaluation of an LLM adversary against four detector modalities on \largeScaleNProjects{} real airdrop campaigns, demonstrating \evasionGraphModerate{}--\evasionGraphAdvanced{}00\% evasion across rules, ML, GBM, and graph detectors (Sections~\ref{sec:evasion},~\ref{sec:graph}).
  \item \textbf{Last-feature-standing analysis.} \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} ranks top-1 in all \behavioralConsistencyTopTotalRounds{} seed-rounds, but its importance converges from 0.36 to 0.17 as the attacker pushes blend ratios to \bestResponseEquilibriumBlendMean{}---quantifying the irreducible residual and explaining why even it approaches irrelevance (Section~\ref{sec:equilibrium}).
  \item \textbf{Closed-loop LLM adversarial training.} A \bestResponseNRounds{}-round closed-loop protocol where Claude Opus sees the detector's top-3 feature importances each round and generates targeted sybils achieves Round-1 evasion \closedLoopRoundLlmEvasionMean{} vs.\ the parametric attacker's \closedLoopRoundParametricEvasionMean{} ($\Delta{=}+$\closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$\times 100$pp), proving that parametric baselines are lower bounds on LLM attacker capability (Section~\ref{sec:closedloop}).
  \item \textbf{Paradigm-shift argument.} We argue that the \bestResponseAucFloorMean{} detection floor is not a call for better classifiers but for a migration to problem-space defenses (execution cost), identity-layer mechanisms (BrightID, Gitcoin Passport, World ID), and full-graph structural analysis (Section~\ref{sec:discussion}).
  \item \textbf{Synthetic-feature audit protocol.} A 3-step checklist (Algorithm~\ref{alg:audit}) for detecting label leakage, validated by our own post-mortem (AUC \leakageFixLeakyAuc{}$\to$\leakageFixHonestAuc{}). Cross-axis LOPO mean AUC \largeScaleCrossAxisGbmMeanAuc{} and independent-label transfer AUC 0.586 establish honest baselines (Sections~\ref{sec:crossaxis},~\ref{sec:leakage}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{HasciDB: The Baseline Detector Under Attack}

HasciDB~\cite{hascidb2026} is a consensus-driven Sybil framework built on five rule-based indicators. Three are \textit{operational}: Burst Transactions (BT), Burst Wallets (BW), and Hop Frequency (HF); two are \textit{fund-flow}: Repeat Funding (RF) and Multi-Account (MA). A wallet is Sybil iff \texttt{ops\_flag = (BT>=5) OR (BW>=10) OR (HF>0.80)} or \texttt{fund\_flag = (RF>0.50) OR (MA>=5)} fires.

HasciDB's Delphi protocol established these thresholds as a 70\%+ consensus among 12 Web3 practitioners; the dataset spans \largeScaleNProjects{} Ethereum L1 airdrops (2020--2024) and 3.6M eligible addresses. In this paper we use a stratified 25k/project subset yielding \largeScaleNAddresses{} addresses, 125,157 Sybils, and an overall Sybil rate of 32.4\%.

\subsection{Adversarial Machine Learning for Security}

Goodfellow et al.~\cite{goodfellow2014} introduced FGSM; Madry et al.~\cite{madry2018} formalized robustness as minimax optimization via PGD-based adversarial training.
Carlini \& Wagner~\cite{carlini2017} showed defenses collapse under stronger optimization; Tramer et al.~\cite{tramer2020} codified adaptive-attack evaluation, requiring full attacker knowledge---a principle we adopt.
Pierazzi et al.~\cite{pierazzi2020} distinguished \textit{feature-space} from \textit{problem-space} attacks: perturbing $\mathbf{x}$ is easier than modifying the real-world object $z$ s.t.\ $\phi(z) = \mathbf{x}$, because problem-space modifications must satisfy domain constraints.
Biggio \& Roli~\cite{biggio2018wild} and Apruzzese et al.~\cite{apruzzese2023} conclude that security-critical systems must assume the attacker knows the feature set. We follow this principle: the LLM attacker receives full knowledge of HasciDB thresholds and AI-feature distributions.

\subsection{Graph-Based Sybil Detection}

Douceur~\cite{douceur2002sybil} proved that Sybil identities cannot be eliminated without a trusted authority. Graph-based defenses exploit structural bottlenecks: SybilGuard~\cite{yu2006sybilguard} uses random-walk convergence, SybilRank~\cite{cao2012sybilrank} improves scalability via short walks from trusted seeds, and SybilBelief~\cite{gong2014sybilbelief} formulates detection as Bayesian inference on a Markov random field. Viswanath et al.~\cite{viswanath2010} show all these rely on sparse honest-Sybil cuts.

In blockchain, TrustaLabs~\cite{trustalabs} applies Louvain~\cite{blondel2008louvain} to asset-transfer graphs; Arbitrum~\cite{arbitrum} deploys Louvain on three graph types; ARTEMIS~\cite{artemis} applies GraphSAGE~\cite{hamilton2017graphsage} to Blur~S2 (AUC 0.803). All assume Sybil wallets exhibit denser internal connectivity---an assumption our LLM adversary targets by generating wallets with low RF and MA values.

\subsection{Sybil Detection in Airdrops: Feature-Based Approaches}

Beyond graph structure, feature-based approaches treat Sybil detection as a supervised classification problem.
HasciDB~\cite{hascidb2026} defines five indicators across two axes: operational (BT, BW, HF) and fund-flow (RF, MA).
Models trained on these indicators achieve near-perfect AUC when the label is derived from the same features---a circularity we call the ``rule-recall trap'' and avoid through cross-axis evaluation.
Our companion Paper~1~\cite{wen2026agent} extends the feature space with 8 execution-layer features that distinguish AI agents from humans at $p < 0.01$ (all 8 features), with Cohen's $d$ up to 1.98 for \texttt{hour\_entropy}~\cite{cohen1988}.
These features are designed to capture behavioral invariants---timing regularity, gas-pricing precision, nonce management---that are orthogonal to HasciDB's threshold-based indicators.

\subsection{LLM as Adversary in Security}

LLMs have been used for red-teaming~\cite{perez2022redteam}, iterative jailbreaking~\cite{chao2023jailbreaking}, phishing~\cite{heiding2024}, penetration testing~\cite{deng2024llm_pentesting}, and autonomous web exploitation~\cite{fang2024llm_agents_attack}.
In the Sybil space, LLMhunter~\cite{llmhunter} uses LLMs for \textit{detection}; we invert this to \textit{generation}---a constrained planning problem requiring the LLM to jointly satisfy multiple evasion criteria while maintaining domain plausibility.

\subsection{Positioning This Work}

This paper is the first to combine an LLM adversary generating adversarial feature vectors, a 4-modality detector benchmark, multi-round adversarial convergence with pre-registered stopping criteria, and a synthetic-feature label-leakage audit.
The closest analogue is adversarial malware generation~\cite{pierazzi2020}, but airdrop detectors operate on 13-dimensional indicator vectors---making evasion easier and defense harder than in the binary-analysis setting.

\subsection{LLM-Based Sybil Generator}
\label{sec:methods:generator}

We implement the generator using \texttt{claude\_agent\_sdk} with Claude Opus 4.6. The generator:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Reads} HasciDB's 5 indicator definitions and the 8 empirical AI feature distributions.
  \item \textbf{Plans one wallet at a time} under a three-tier sophistication ladder (Table~\ref{tab:tiers}).
  \item \textbf{Emits JSON} with 13 numeric fields and a natural-language rationale.
  \item \textbf{Validates thresholds} before accepting: $\texttt{BT}{<}5 \wedge \texttt{BW}{<}10 \wedge \texttt{HF}{<}0.8 \wedge \texttt{RF}{<}0.5 \wedge \texttt{MA}{<}5$.
  \item \textbf{Caches} all LLM responses by SHA-256 for reproducibility.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Prompt structure.}
The system prompt contains three components: (1)~a \textit{context block} providing HasciDB's indicator definitions, thresholds, and mean/std of each feature from the non-Sybil population; (2)~a \textit{role instruction} directing the LLM to act as a wallet operator planning a single wallet's on-chain footprint; and (3)~a \textit{constraint block} specifying the sophistication tier and output format.
We do not include the full prompt to avoid enabling direct replication for malicious purposes (\S\ref{sec:ethics}), but we release the prompt template with the context block redacted.

\noindent\textbf{Sophistication tiers.}
Table~\ref{tab:tiers} details the three tiers. The key insight is that the difficulty for detectors is \textit{non-monotonic} across tiers: the advanced tier produces internally consistent wallets that are paradoxically easier to detect than moderate-tier wallets on some features, because the LLM's ``too clean'' distributions lack the noise of real human traffic.

\subsection{Cross-Axis Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:methods:crossaxis}

The central methodological move is to \textit{never} train on the same features the label was derived from.
HasciDB's joint label is defined as $y = \text{ops\_flag} \lor \text{fund\_flag}$, where $\text{ops\_flag} = (\text{BT}{\ge}5) \lor (\text{BW}{\ge}10) \lor (\text{HF}{>}0.8)$ and $\text{fund\_flag} = (\text{RF}{>}0.5) \lor (\text{MA}{\ge}5)$.
Training a classifier $f(\text{BT}, \text{BW}, \text{HF}, \text{RF}, \text{MA}) \to y$ is tautological: the model merely reconstructs the rule, yielding AUC $\approx 1.0$ that reflects rule fidelity, not detection generalization.
We term this the \textit{rule-recall trap} and avoid it via three orthogonal evaluation strategies:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cross-axis LOPO.} Train on OPS features $\{$BT,BW,HF$\}$ to predict \texttt{fund\_flag} (derived only from $\{$RF,MA$\}$), and vice versa, in a 16-fold leave-one-project-out loop.
  Because the feature set and the label-derivation set are disjoint, any AUC above 0.5 reflects genuine statistical dependence between operational and fund-flow Sybil behavior---i.e., the \textit{transferable} signal.
  \item \textbf{Independent-label transfer.} Train on HasciDB's 5 indicators to predict Gitcoin FDD's independently curated Sybil list (which does not use any of the 5 indicators in its methodology).
  This tests whether HasciDB's behavioral features generalize to an external Sybil definition.
  \item \textbf{Honest binary classification.} Classify (LLM Sybil) vs (real HasciDB wallet) using only the 8 AI features from Paper~1, eliminating circular labels entirely.
  The task is well-defined because the label is exogenous: LLM sybils are synthetic, not derived from any feature.
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textbf{GBM hyperparameters.}
All gradient-boosted models use LightGBM~\cite{ke2017lightgbm} with 150 estimators, max depth~4, learning rate~0.1, subsample~0.8, and random seed~42.
Hyperparameters were selected via 5-fold CV on the 1inch project (the largest by Sybil rate) and held fixed across all \largeScaleNProjects{} projects to avoid overfitting to project-specific patterns.
Evaluation uses 5-fold stratified CV within each LOPO test fold, reporting mean AUC and standard deviation.

\subsection{Graph-Based Detection via Louvain Community Detection}
\label{sec:methods:graph}

To evaluate structural defenses, we construct a funding-flow proxy graph $G = (V, E)$ from HasciDB's RF and MA indicators across all \largeScaleNProjects{} projects (up to 8,000 addresses per project).

\noindent\textbf{Graph construction.}
Let $V$ be the set of addresses for a given project.
For each address $v \in V$ with $\text{RF}(v) \ge 0.3$, we create a synthetic funding hub $h_v$ and add edges $(h_v, v)$; addresses sharing a hub form a star topology representing common funding sources.
For each set of addresses $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ where $\text{MA}(v_i) \ge 3$ for all $i$, we form a clique, representing multi-account clusters.
Non-sybil addresses receive $\text{Poisson}(1.5)$ random edges to model organic transfer activity.
Formally, the edge set is:
\begin{align}
E = E_{\text{RF}} \cup E_{\text{MA}} \cup E_{\text{random}}
\end{align}
where $|E_{\text{RF}}|$ scales linearly with the number of high-RF addresses and $|E_{\text{MA}}|$ scales quadratically with the MA cluster size.
Across \largeScaleNProjects{} projects, the resulting graphs contain 8,000--9,800 nodes and 12,100--21,400 edges (Table~\ref{tab:graph_stats}).

\noindent\textbf{Detection.}
We apply Louvain community detection~\cite{blondel2008louvain} with resolution parameter $\gamma{=}1.0$, which maximizes the modularity $Q = \frac{1}{2m}\sum_{ij}\left[A_{ij} - \gamma\frac{k_ik_j}{2m}\right]\delta(c_i, c_j)$ where $A$ is the adjacency matrix, $k_i$ is the degree of node $i$, $m = |E|$, and $c_i$ is the community assignment.
A community $C$ is flagged as Sybil if $\frac{|\{v \in C : \text{fund\_flag}(v)=1\}|}{|C|} > 0.4$ and $|C| \ge 3$.

This proxy is \textit{conservative}: real graph detectors (SybilRank~\cite{cao2012sybilrank}, SybilBelief~\cite{gong2014sybilbelief}) operate on the full transfer graph with substantially more signal, so our evasion rates may represent upper bounds on what an LLM adversary achieves against production graph detectors.

\subsection{Adversarial Convergence Protocol}
\label{sec:methods:equilibrium}

We implement a \bestResponseNRounds{}-round best-response protocol with blend selection, repeated over \bestResponseNSeeds{} seeds (42, 137, 314):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Round 0:} Train a GBM detector (150 trees, depth 4, lr 0.1) on 15,000 real wallets + 3,000 LLM sybils at human-blend ratio 0.45.
  \item \textbf{Round $t+1$:} Probe the current detector with 500 sybils at each of 14 blend ratios ($0.30, 0.35, \ldots, 0.95$); select the blend that maximizes evasion rate (the attacker's \emph{best response}). Generate 3,000 new LLM sybils at this blend; retrain the detector on all cumulative sybils + 15,000 real wallets; measure AUC, evasion rate, and feature importances.
  \item \textbf{Convergence criterion:} $|\Delta\text{AUC}|{<}0.01$ for 3 consecutive rounds.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Synthetic-Feature Audit Protocol}
\label{sec:methods:audit}

Any paper mixing real features with synthetic auxiliary features on real labels risks label leakage. We formalize a 3-step audit as Algorithm~\ref{alg:audit}, validated by our own post-mortem (\S\ref{sec:leakage}).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Synthetic-Feature Audit Checklist}
\label{alg:audit}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Dataset $\mathcal{D}$ with real features $\mathbf{X}_r$, synthetic features $\mathbf{X}_s$, label $y$
\STATE \textbf{Step 1 (Independence).} Verify that the sampling distribution $P(\mathbf{X}_s)$ does \textit{not} condition on $y$. If $\mathbf{X}_s \sim P(\cdot \mid y)$, the pipeline leaks labels.
\STATE \textbf{Step 2 (Auxiliary-only test).} Train a classifier on $\mathbf{X}_s$ alone to predict $y$. If AUC $\gg 0.5$, the synthetic features contain label information.
\STATE \textbf{Step 3 (Leakage-free ablation).} Redefine the task to avoid the circular label (e.g., classify LLM sybils vs.\ real wallets) and re-run the feature ablation on this leakage-free task before reporting main-task numbers.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\noindent\textbf{Why evasion is trivial for rule-based detectors.}
HasciDB's five indicators are all threshold-based: a wallet is flagged iff any indicator exceeds a published cutoff.
An LLM that reads these thresholds can trivially plan below them.
This is not a weakness of the specific thresholds---it is a fundamental limitation of any detection system that publishes its decision boundary in closed form.
The cross-axis GBM is harder to evade (2.1\% detection on basic) because it has learned correlations \textit{between} indicators that the adversary must jointly satisfy, but even this correlation structure is defeated when the LLM plans all features jointly (advanced tier: 0.1\% detection).

The \evasionThresholdRules{}00\% evasion rate against HasciDB's threshold rules is \emph{expected}: the LLM receives the exact threshold values in its prompt and can trivially generate values below each cutoff. The non-trivial finding is not the evasion itself but the \emph{quality} of the evasion: the LLM's boundary-hugging strategy (55\% of samples at BT=4, BW=9, HF=0.79) and tight AI-feature clustering (IQR~0.028) create distinctive distributional signatures that a population-level detector could exploit. Perfect evasion of \emph{individual} thresholds does not guarantee evasion of \emph{distributional} tests.

A three-condition blind test confirms the threat is not threshold-memorization. Under \emph{full knowledge} (exact thresholds provided), evasion is 100\%. Under \emph{partial knowledge} (detection methods described qualitatively, no thresholds), evasion remains 100\%---the LLM infers safe ranges from descriptions alone. Under \emph{zero knowledge} (`generate realistic DeFi wallets,' no mention of detection), evasion is still 61\%, with only the BT indicator triggering (39\% of wallets, mean BT=4.08 near the $\geq$5 threshold). The knowledge--evasion gradient (100\%$\to$100\%$\to$61\%) demonstrates that the LLM's world-knowledge of legitimate DeFi behavior is itself substantially adversarial to threshold-based detectors.

\subsection{Closed-Loop LLM Adversarial Training}
\label{sec:closedloop}

The best-response protocol of \S\ref{sec:equilibrium} uses a \emph{parametric} attacker that selects a scalar blend ratio; the attacker's strategy space is a one-dimensional family.
A real LLM adversary is not so constrained.
We therefore run a \emph{closed-loop} protocol in which Claude Opus 4.6 receives the detector's top-3 feature importances and current evasion rate after every round, and generates 100 targeted sybils per round---not by blending, but by \textit{planning against the detector's weaknesses}.

\noindent\textbf{Protocol.}
Each closed-loop run proceeds over \bestResponseNRounds{} rounds with \bestResponseNSeeds{} seeds (42, 137, 314).
Round~0 bootstraps from the parametric attacker (100 sybils at blend 0.45).
In each subsequent round, the LLM receives:
(1)~the detector's top-3 feature importances,
(2)~the previous round's evasion rate, and
(3)~the full feature-space constraints (HasciDB thresholds + AI-feature distributions).
It then generates 100 targeted sybil feature vectors.
The detector retrains on all cumulative sybils plus 15,000 real wallets.
A scale-matched parametric control runs in parallel with the same number of sybils per round but using scalar-blend generation.
In total the experiment involves 30 batch API calls across \bestResponseNSeeds{} seeds, generating ${\sim}$3,000 LLM sybils, completing in 81 minutes at a cost of ${\sim}$\$30--50.

\noindent\textbf{Head-to-head comparison at Round~1.}
The critical comparison uses the \emph{same detector} $D_1$ (trained on Round-0 data) and measures evasion of LLM-generated vs.\ parametric sybils.
Table~\ref{tab:closedloop} reports per-round results (\bestResponseNSeeds{}-seed mean).
At Round~1, the LLM achieves evasion rate $\closedLoopRoundLlmEvasionMean{}$ vs.\ the parametric attacker's $\closedLoopRoundParametricEvasionMean{}$---a gap of $+$\closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$\times 100$ percentage points ($\Delta = \closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$).
This gap is the central finding: \textbf{parametric approximations understate real-world LLM attacker capability by nearly 30 percentage points.}

The +\closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$\times 100$\,pp gap is a \emph{transient, first-round} advantage: by Round-10, both attackers converge to similar AUC (closed-loop \closedLoopFinalAucMean{} vs.\ parametric 0.982, $\Delta{=}$+1.0\,pp). The $+$\closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$\times 100$pp evasion gap at Round~1 means that all prior parametric adversarial ML benchmarks---including our own best-response protocol (\S\ref{sec:equilibrium})---are \emph{lower bounds} on attacker capability.
Defenses calibrated to parametric attackers will fail against LLM attackers that can read feature importances and plan targeted evasion.
This is, to our knowledge, the first closed-loop LLM adversarial training result on structured feature-space detectors.

\subsection{Problem-Space Pilot}
\label{sec:problemspace}

\textbf{Note:} This is a dry-run feasibility study; no transactions were executed on-chain. The results estimate feasibility gaps but do not validate real-world executability.

To assess the feasibility gap between feature-space and problem-space attacks, we conducted a dry-run pilot deploying 10 LLM-planned wallets through a simulated transaction executor that models gas pricing, nonce management, and inter-transaction timing.

\noindent\textbf{Protocol.}
For each of 10 wallets, the LLM generates a complete transaction plan: (1)~a target feature vector (13 values), (2)~a sequence of 50--200 simulated transactions with timestamps, gas prices, and target contracts, and (3)~a verification step where the simulator recomputes all 13 features from the executed transaction sequence.
The feasibility gap is measured as the relative error $|\hat{x}_i - x_i^*| / x_i^*$ between the planned feature value $x_i^*$ and the realized value $\hat{x}_i$.

\noindent\textbf{Results.}
All 13 features achieved $<$8.3\% relative gap between planned and actual values. Integer features (BT, BW, MA) matched exactly; the worst continuous gap was \texttt{response\_latency\_variance} at 8.3\%.
This confirms high-fidelity feature-to-problem-space translation for isolated wallets, though two caveats apply: the simulator omits gas-price market dynamics and cross-wallet population homogeneity, both of which may introduce detectable signatures at scale.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

% FLUID: Discussion of floor value
\subsection{\bestResponseAucFloorMean{} Is a Floor, Not a Score}

The central result is \textbf{\bestResponseAucFloorMean{}}---the convergence point of best-response adversarial training in the 8-feature behavioral space. Three lines of evidence support interpreting this as a \emph{practical detection floor}:
\textbf{(1)}~AUC converges to $\bestResponseAucFloorMean{} \pm \bestResponseAucFloorStd{}$ across \bestResponseNSeeds{} seeds with $|\Delta\text{AUC}|{<}0.01$ for the last 3 rounds; a fixed-schedule protocol yields \equilibriumFinalAuc{}, confirming the result is not a schedule artifact.
\textbf{(2)}~At blend ${\sim}\bestResponseEquilibriumBlendMean{}$, sybils share 93\% of their distributional mass with real wallets; the residual 0.078 above chance concentrates in \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} (CV of inter-arrival times), a structural invariant the LLM cannot replicate without real-time traffic modeling.
\textbf{(3)}~Feature-importance Gini drops from 0.54 to 0.15, indicating the detector has exhausted all 8 dimensions.

This is a practical observation, not a theoretical optimality proof. Fundamentally different features could exceed this floor, but within the 8-feature space, multiple starting conditions converge to the same point: \emph{change the problem}, do not keep optimizing within the bounded paradigm.

\subsection{Game-Theoretic Framing}

The protocol maps to a Stackelberg game~\cite{bruckner2011stackelberg,osborne1994game}:
\begin{align}
\min_{\theta} \max_{\phi} \; & \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\phi} \left[ \mathbf{1}[f_\theta(x) < \tau] \right] \label{eq:stackelberg} \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & x_i \in [\ell_i, u_i], \; \text{BT}{<}5, \; \text{BW}{<}10, \; \text{HF}{<}0.8 \nonumber
\end{align}
where $f_\theta$ is the detector, $p_\phi$ the attacker distribution, and $\tau$ the threshold. Convergence to AUC $\bestResponseAucFloorMean{} \pm \bestResponseAucFloorStd{}$ is consistent with an approximate equilibrium, though this is a heuristic best-response over a discrete grid, not a continuous optimization with provable guarantees.

% FLUID: Defense paradigm recommendations depend on floor value
\subsection{What Must Replace Feature-Space Detection}

The \bestResponseAucFloorMean{} floor motivates investment in mechanisms outside the attacker's optimization surface:

\noindent\textbf{1. Problem-space defenses.}
Feature-space attacks cost \$12 in API credits; problem-space execution costs 0.1--0.5~ETH per wallet (\$200--\$1,000 at ${\sim}$30~Gwei). Protocols should require expensive on-chain execution that cannot be planned in a single prompt~\cite{pierazzi2020}.

\noindent\textbf{2. Identity-layer defenses.}
BrightID, Gitcoin Passport, and World~ID establish human uniqueness orthogonally to behavioral features. An LLM can mimic transactions but cannot produce a retinal scan.

\noindent\textbf{3. Full-graph structural analysis.}
Our real transfer-graph evaluation (\S\ref{sec:real-graph}) shows Louvain achieves NMI 0.04--0.07 against sybil labels, indicating that community-detection graph approaches also face limits. Defense must weight identity and execution-cost layers heavily.

\noindent\textbf{4. Ensemble defense.}
Under independence, joint evasion $p_1 \cdot p_2 \cdot p_3 \approx 0.20$ across behavioral ($p_1{\approx}0.42$), graph ($p_2{\approx}0.94$), and temporal ($p_3{\approx}0.50$) layers. Layered defenses are necessary, not optional.

% FLUID: Closed-loop comparison numbers
\subsection{Parametric Baselines Understate Risk}

The closed-loop experiment (\S\ref{sec:closedloop}) shows that Round-1 LLM evasion (\closedLoopRoundLlmEvasionMean{}) exceeds parametric evasion (\closedLoopRoundParametricEvasionMean{}) by $+$\closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$\times 100$pp against the \emph{same} detector.
The scalar-blend family is a one-dimensional subspace of the LLM's strategy space; the LLM targets specific feature combinations that exploit decision-boundary geometry. Prior LLM adversarial work operates in NL or code domains~\cite{chao2023jailbreaking,deng2024llm_pentesting}; our protocol is the first on structured numerical features. The practical recommendation: adversarial evaluation must include closed-loop LLM attackers, not only parametric baselines.

% FLUID: Reconciliation of two AUC numbers
\subsection{Reconciling the \bestResponseAucFloorMean{} Floor and \closedLoopFinalAucMean{} Closed-Loop AUC}

The apparent contradiction between the parametric floor (\bestResponseAucFloorMean{}) and the closed-loop detector AUC (\closedLoopFinalAucMean{}) resolves once we distinguish attacker and defender perspectives. The \bestResponseAucFloorMean{} measures the parametric attacker's ability to degrade the detector under best-response convergence; the \closedLoopFinalAucMean{} measures the retrained detector's ability to classify a fixed pool of LLM-generated sybils after \bestResponseNRounds{} rounds of closed-loop training. The LLM attacker is harder to evade initially ($+$\closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$\times 100$pp at Round~1) but its sybils are more homogeneous---generated from a single model under fixed prompting constraints---allowing the detector to specialize. The meaningful comparison is: against a static detector (no retraining), the LLM achieves 98\% evasion while the parametric attacker achieves 69\%. Against an adaptive detector (retraining each round), both converge to high AUC. The practical threat therefore depends on retraining frequency: defenders who retrain after each batch of claims neutralize the LLM advantage within ${\sim}$4 rounds, but most deployed systems do not retrain at this cadence.

\subsection{Reconciling Cross-Paper Numbers}

Paper~0's Fano ceiling (97.2\%) characterizes the information content of 31 behavioral features under i.i.d.\ evaluation. Paper~1's temporal holdout (AUC~0.67) measures operational accuracy under distribution shift. Our detection floor (\bestResponseAucFloorMean{}) measures feature-space detection under adversarial pressure on 8 AI-specific features. These three numbers are not contradictory: they measure different dimensions of the same problem (information content, temporal robustness, and adversarial robustness, respectively). Together they paint a complete picture: the features \emph{contain} the signal (Fano), but the signal \emph{degrades} over time (temporal) and \emph{erodes} under attack (adversarial).

\subsection{Why \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} Survives}

\texttt{behavioral\_consistency} ranks top-1 in all \behavioralConsistencyTopInNRounds{}/\behavioralConsistencyTopTotalRounds{} seed-rounds because it measures the CV of inter-arrival times---a higher-order temporal statistic requiring realistic skewness, kurtosis, and autocorrelation that a single JSON prompt cannot achieve, unlike \texttt{hour\_entropy} (a 24-bin histogram). Even so, its importance declines from 0.36 to 0.17: it is converging toward irrelevance.

% FLUID: External validation numbers
\paragraph{Honest Floor and External Validation.}
Cross-axis LOPO mean AUC \largeScaleCrossAxisGbmMeanAuc{} is the honest floor for HasciDB; any detector reporting AUC~$>$0.95 on the 5-indicator label is measuring rule recall, not generalization.

External cross-method validation using independently-created sybil lists (HOP Protocol 14,195 addresses; LayerZero 21,395 addresses) reveals near-zero inter-method agreement: HasciDB--HOP $\kappa{=}\externalHopKappa{}$, HasciDB--LayerZero $\kappa{=}\externalLayerzeroKappa{}$, HOP--Gitcoin\_FDD $\kappa{=}{-}0.40$ (anti-correlated). Different detection methods see fundamentally different sybil populations. LLM-generated sybils (0 matches across all external lists) operate in the shared blind spot.

Further, only 1 of 15 airdrop projects (Gitcoin) published an official sybil list. Of Gitcoin FDD's \officialSybilGitcoinOfficialCount{} flagged addresses, only \officialSybilOverlapInHascidb{} overlap with HasciDB's 23,878-address universe; among those, HasciDB catches 61 (\officialSybilHascidbCatchRate{}$\times 100$\% recall). The near-absence of official ground truth across the industry underscores that sybil detection lacks consensus not only across methods but across the projects themselves.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:ethics}

This work raises dual-use concerns that we address directly.
We shared evasion results with the HasciDB team 60 days before public release (March 2026), giving them time to incorporate adversarial training. We do not release the full LLM generation prompt; only a redacted template is provided. Cached JSON feature vectors are released for reproducibility.

The key attack ingredients---HasciDB's thresholds and indicator definitions---are already publicly available. Our contribution is not to enable the attack but to \textit{measure the ceiling} and \textit{redirect defense investment}. The convergence result tells protocol teams to shift resources from classifier tuning to structurally stronger defenses.

The detection floor likely generalizes beyond airdrops: any system relying on behavioral heuristics in a low-dimensional feature space with a narrow problem-space gap may face a similar convergence under LLM attack. Anti-fraud systems, spam filters, and bot detectors should conduct adversarial convergence analysis to determine their own practical floors.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\noindent\textbf{1. Feature-space attack, not problem-space.}
The LLM outputs JSON feature vectors, not real on-chain transactions. While our pilot (\S\ref{sec:problemspace}) shows $<$8.3\% relative gap between planned and simulated feature values, a full problem-space attack would require funding each wallet from a unique source (to avoid RF clustering), spacing transactions realistically, and paying real gas costs (0.1--0.5~ETH per wallet at ${\sim}$30~Gwei). A full testnet deployment with HasciDB recomputation remains the critical next step.

\noindent\textbf{2. Proxy graph, not full transfer graph.}
Our Louvain evaluation uses a funding-flow proxy graph from HasciDB's RF and MA indicators, not the raw Ethereum transfer graph. The proxy contains 8,000--10,000 nodes per project, whereas the full graph may have $10\times$--$100\times$ more edges. Real graph detectors (SybilRank~\cite{cao2012sybilrank}, SybilBelief~\cite{gong2014sybilbelief}, GNNs~\cite{artemis}) operate on this richer graph and likely achieve higher recall; our evasion rates (\evasionGraphModerate{}0--\evasionGraphAdvanced{}00\%) should be interpreted as upper bounds.

\noindent\textbf{3. Single chain (Ethereum L1 only).}
All \largeScaleNProjects{} projects are Ethereum L1 airdrops. L2 rollups differ in gas mechanics (10--100$\times$ cheaper), transaction throughput, and sequencer-imposed timing, all of which may alter AI-feature distributions. An L2 replication study is needed.

\noindent\textbf{4. Single LLM model (Claude Opus 4.6 only).}
Different models may produce different evasion profiles; open-source models could be fine-tuned specifically for Sybil generation. A multi-model adversary benchmark is needed.

\noindent\textbf{5. Heuristic convergence.}
The best-response protocol uses a discrete 14-point blend grid, not continuous optimization with provable guarantees. The convergence-point spread across seeds is \bestResponseAucFloorStd{}$\times 2$ AUC; schedule choice affects the endpoint by ${\sim}$0.02.

\noindent\textbf{6. 4-project leakage-fix subset.}
The label-leakage analysis (\S\ref{sec:leakage}) uses a 4-project subset; the full \largeScaleNProjects{}-project rerun is pending but expected to be qualitatively identical (the leakage mechanism is project-independent).

\noindent\textbf{7. What would change our conclusions.}
The floor claim would weaken if: (a)~a full-graph detector achieves $\ge$50\% recall on LLM sybils; (b)~maintaining \texttt{behavioral\_consistency}~$<$~0.2 proves infeasible at scale; or (c)~new behavioral features push equilibrium AUC above 0.70. None would eliminate the fundamental tension, but any would raise the convergence point.

\noindent\textbf{Future work.}
(a)~Full-graph adversarial evaluation with SybilRank, SybilBelief, and GNN detectors on actual Ethereum transfer graphs.
(b)~Problem-space attacks on an Ethereum testnet measuring actual gas costs of achieving blend ratio $\ge$0.90.
(c)~Identity-layer integration: measuring incremental AUC from Gitcoin Passport or World~ID attestations.
(d)~Extending closed-loop adversarial training with RL-style reward signals beyond feature importances.
(e)~L2 replication across Arbitrum, Optimism, and Base.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

% FLUID: Conclusion restates key numbers
Our most important number is \textbf{\bestResponseAucFloorMean{}}---the empirical convergence point of best-response adversarial training, establishing a practical detection floor for feature-space sybil detection under adaptive LLM attack.

This result rests on three pillars. First, cross-modality evasion is near-total: LLM sybils evade rules (\evasionThresholdRules{}00\%), ML (\evasionThresholdRules{}00\%), GBM (\evasionLightgbmMl{}0\%), and graph detection (\evasionGraphModerate{}--\evasionGraphAdvanced{}00\%) at \$12 in API cost. Second, a \bestResponseNRounds{}-round best-response protocol converges to AUC $\bestResponseAucFloorMean{} \pm \bestResponseAucFloorStd{}$ across \bestResponseNSeeds{} seeds, with \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} as the last feature standing (top-1 in \behavioralConsistencyTopInNRounds{}/\behavioralConsistencyTopTotalRounds{} seed-rounds) yet converging toward irrelevance at blend ratio ${\sim}\bestResponseEquilibriumBlendMean{}$. Third, closed-loop LLM adversarial training achieves Round-1 evasion \closedLoopRoundLlmEvasionMean{} vs.\ the parametric \closedLoopRoundParametricEvasionMean{} ($\Delta{=}+$\closedLoopRoundEvasionDelta{}$\times 100$pp), proving parametric benchmarks are lower bounds on attacker capability.

The detection floor should redirect the sybil detection community from building better classifiers to building costlier execution environments, identity-layer attestation (BrightID, Gitcoin Passport, World~ID), and full-graph structural analysis. We also contribute a 3-step leakage-audit checklist (AUC \leakageFixLeakyAuc{}$\to$\leakageFixHonestAuc{} post-fix) and cross-axis baselines (LOPO AUC \largeScaleCrossAxisGbmMeanAuc{}).

The broader lesson extends beyond airdrops: any detection system operating on behavioral features in a low-dimensional space with a narrow problem-space gap likely faces a similar convergence under adaptive LLM attack. Closed-loop LLM adversarial training---not parametric best-response---should become the standard evaluation for security-critical classifiers.
