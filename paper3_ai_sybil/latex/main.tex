\title{LLM-as-Adversary: A Cross-Modality Benchmark for AI Sybil Evasion in Airdrop Detection}
\author{NSF Project Team}
\affiliation{\institution{}\country{USA}}

\begin{abstract}
We present the first end-to-end evaluation of LLM-driven Sybil evasion against production-grade airdrop detection across four detector modalities.
Using a Claude Opus 4.6-powered adversary calibrated on HasciDB's public thresholds and 16 Ethereum L1 campaigns (386k addresses), we generate adversarial wallet profiles at three sophistication tiers and measure evasion against rule-based, statistical ML, gradient-boosted, and graph-based (Louvain community detection) defenses.
\textbf{Cross-modality threat.}
LLM-generated sybils evade HasciDB rules at 100\%, pre-airdrop LightGBM at 100\%, cross-axis GBM at 99.9\%, and Louvain graph detection at 94.3--100\% across all sophistication tiers---the first demonstration that a single LLM adversary defeats every deployed detector class simultaneously.
\textbf{Behavioral defense.}
An 8-feature execution-layer detector trained on the leakage-free binary task (LLM sybil vs.\ real wallet) achieves AUC 0.987 on the hardest adversary tier, with \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} as the most robust single feature.
\textbf{Adversarial equilibrium.}
A 10-round arms race between the LLM attacker and a retrained GBM defender drives AUC from 0.985 to 0.599 ($\Delta\text{AUC}{<}0.01$ at round~9), with evasion rate crossing detection rate at round~6 and \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} ranking top-1 in all 10 rounds---consistent with a Stackelberg equilibrium.
\textbf{Methodological contribution.}
We document a label-leakage bug in our own pilot (AUC 0.953$\to$0.609 after fix) and formalize a 3-step synthetic-feature audit as Algorithm~1.
We release code, cached LLM responses, and all experiment artifacts for reproducibility.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Motivation}

Airdrops are the default retroactive-reward mechanism for Ethereum-adjacent protocols, distributing hundreds of millions of dollars per campaign to users that satisfy eligibility heuristics. HasciDB~\cite{hascidb2026} is the most ambitious public audit of these distributions: using a modified-Delphi consensus of 12 Web3 practitioners it fixes five behavioral indicators---Burst Transactions (BT $\ge 5$), Burst Wallets (BW $\ge 10$), Hop Frequency (HF $\ge 0.80$), Repeat Funding (RF $\ge 0.50$) and Multi-Account (MA $\ge 5$)---and applies them to 3.6M eligible addresses across 16 Ethereum L1 campaigns (2020--2024), flagging roughly 30\% as Sybil.

All prior work shares a tacit assumption: \textit{the adversary runs a deterministic script}. The five indicators and the supervised models that learn them are fingerprints for classical batch-automation. They do not speak to an adversary that can \textit{read the rule} and \textit{plan against it}.

Meanwhile, LLM-controlled on-chain agents have moved from curiosity to commodity. Our companion Paper~1~\cite{wen2026agent} labels 2,590 addresses as agents and 726 as humans and extracts 8 execution-layer features where all satisfy $p{<}0.01$ in Mann-Whitney U, with Cohen's $d$ up to 1.98 (\texttt{hour\_entropy}). If an attacker can automate these agents and teach them to read HasciDB's thresholds, can HasciDB still see them? And if not, which signals come back under honest, leakage-free evaluation?

\subsection{Attacker Model}

Throughout this paper the adversary is an LLM-powered wallet operator with:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Full read access} to HasciDB's 5 indicator definitions and thresholds (public in~\cite{hascidb2026}).
  \item \textbf{Access to empirical AI-feature distributions} from Paper~1 (also public).
  \item \textbf{Per-wallet planning} via \texttt{claude\_agent\_sdk} (Claude Opus 4.6), producing a JSON feature vector per wallet.
  \item \textbf{No live gas-token budget constraint} during evaluation; we measure evasion against feature-space detectors, not on-chain costs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Research Questions}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{RQ1 (Baseline honesty).} What is the leakage-free performance of HasciDB rule-based and ML detectors across 16 projects under cross-axis evaluation?
  \item \textbf{RQ2 (Cross-modality evasion).} Do LLM-generated sybils evade all four detector modalities---rules, statistical ML, gradient-boosted ML, and graph-based community detection---simultaneously?
  \item \textbf{RQ3 (Behavioral defense).} Can execution-layer behavioral features detect LLM sybils under a leakage-free binary evaluation?
  \item \textbf{RQ4 (Adversarial equilibrium).} Does a multi-round arms race between attacker and defender converge, and which features remain robust?
  \item \textbf{RQ5 (Feature ablation).} In the honest binary task, which of the 8 AI features carry signal individually, in combination, and under leave-one-feature-out?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Contributions}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Cross-modality evasion benchmark.} The first evaluation of an LLM adversary against four detector modalities on 16 real airdrop campaigns, demonstrating 94--100\% evasion across rules, ML, GBM, and graph detectors (Sections~\ref{sec:evasion},~\ref{sec:graph}).
  \item \textbf{Behavioral fingerprinting defense.} An 8-feature execution-layer detector achieving AUC 0.987 on the hardest adversary tier in a leakage-free binary task, with \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} as the most robust feature (Section~\ref{sec:binary}).
  \item \textbf{Adversarial equilibrium analysis.} A 10-round arms race converging to AUC $\approx$0.60 with $\Delta\text{AUC}{<}0.01$ at round~9, with \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} ranking top-1 in 10/10 rounds (Section~\ref{sec:equilibrium}).
  \item \textbf{Synthetic-feature audit protocol.} A 3-step checklist (Algorithm~\ref{alg:audit}) for detecting label leakage in hybrid real/synthetic feature pipelines, validated by our own post-mortem (Section~\ref{sec:methods:audit}).
  \item \textbf{Honest baselines and post-mortem.} Cross-axis LOPO mean AUC 0.617, independent-label transfer AUC 0.586, and transparent retraction of a leaked AUC 0.953$\to$0.609 (Sections~\ref{sec:crossaxis},~\ref{sec:leakage}).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{HasciDB: The Baseline Detector Under Attack}

HasciDB~\cite{hascidb2026} is a consensus-driven Sybil framework built on five rule-based indicators. Two are \textit{operational}: Burst Transactions (BT), Burst Wallets (BW), and Hop Frequency (HF); two are \textit{fund-flow}: Repeat Funding (RF) and Multi-Account (MA). A wallet is Sybil iff \texttt{ops\_flag = (BT>=5) OR (BW>=10) OR (HF>0.80)} or \texttt{fund\_flag = (RF>0.50) OR (MA>=5)} fires.

HasciDB's Delphi protocol established these thresholds as a 70\%+ consensus among 12 Web3 practitioners; the dataset spans 16 Ethereum L1 airdrops (2020--2024) and 3.6M eligible addresses. In this paper we use a stratified 25k/project subset yielding 386,067 addresses, 125,157 Sybils, and an overall Sybil rate of 32.4\%.

\subsection{Adversarial Machine Learning for Security}

The adversarial ML literature provides the threat model for this work.
Goodfellow et al.~\cite{goodfellow2014} introduced FGSM attacks and showed that small $\ell_\infty$ perturbations fool deep classifiers; Kurakin et al.~\cite{kurakin2017adversarial} extended these to physical-world inputs.
Madry et al.~\cite{madry2018} formalized adversarial robustness as a minimax optimization: $\min_\theta \max_{\|\delta\|\le\epsilon} \mathcal{L}(f_\theta(x+\delta), y)$, establishing PGD-based adversarial training as the gold standard.
Carlini \& Wagner~\cite{carlini2017} demonstrated that evaluation standards matter: defenses that appear robust under weak attacks collapse under stronger optimization.
Tramer et al.~\cite{tramer2020} codified adaptive-attack evaluation, requiring that the attacker be given full knowledge of the defense---a principle we adopt.

Crucially, Pierazzi et al.~\cite{pierazzi2020} distinguished \textit{feature-space} from \textit{problem-space} attacks: perturbing a feature vector $\mathbf{x}$ is strictly easier than modifying the real-world object $z$ such that $\phi(z) = \mathbf{x}$, because problem-space modifications must satisfy domain constraints (e.g., valid transactions, gas costs).
Biggio \& Roli~\cite{biggio2018wild} surveyed a decade of adversarial ML, concluding that security-critical deployments must assume the attacker knows the feature set.
Apruzzese et al.~\cite{apruzzese2023} extended this analysis to cybersecurity, where feature-space assumptions are often violated.
Our work sits squarely in this tradition: we give the LLM attacker full knowledge of both HasciDB thresholds and AI-feature distributions, and we separately assess problem-space feasibility (\S\ref{sec:problemspace}).

\subsection{Graph-Based Sybil Detection}

The Sybil attack was formalized by Douceur~\cite{douceur2002sybil}, who proved that without a trusted central authority, Sybil identities cannot be eliminated.
Graph-based defenses exploit the structural bottleneck between honest and Sybil regions.
\textbf{SybilGuard}~\cite{yu2006sybilguard} uses random-walk convergence in social graphs, bounding Sybil acceptance to $O(\sqrt{n}\log n)$.
\textbf{SybilRank}~\cite{cao2012sybilrank} improves scalability via short random walks from trusted seeds, achieving $O(n\log n)$ complexity and deployment at Tuenti (the largest Spanish social network at the time, with 12M users).
\textbf{SybilBelief}~\cite{gong2014sybilbelief} formulates detection as Bayesian inference on a Markov random field, propagating posterior probabilities from labeled seeds.
Viswanath et al.~\cite{viswanath2010} provide a unifying analysis showing that all random-walk-based detectors rely on the same mixing-time assumption: that honest-Sybil cut edges are sparse.

In the blockchain context, community-detection methods have become dominant.
\textbf{TrustaLabs}~\cite{trustalabs} applies Louvain~\cite{blondel2008louvain} and K-Core decomposition to asset-transfer graphs, flagging communities with high internal transfer density.
The \textbf{Arbitrum Foundation}~\cite{arbitrum} deploys Louvain on three graph types (direct transfer, same-funder, and temporal co-activity) and reports sybil cluster sizes ranging from 3 to 4,000 addresses.
\textbf{ARTEMIS}~\cite{artemis} moves beyond community detection, applying a GraphSAGE-based GNN~\cite{hamilton2017graphsage} to the Blur S2 campaign (AUC 0.803).
Pre-airdrop detection (Wen et al.~\cite{wen2026preairdrop}) reports AUC 0.793 at $T{-}30$ days using LightGBM on temporal features derived from the same indicator space.

A key limitation shared by all graph-based approaches is the assumption that Sybil wallets exhibit denser internal connectivity than honest wallets.
Our LLM adversary specifically targets this assumption by generating wallets with low RF and MA values, thereby avoiding the funding-flow clusters that Louvain identifies.

\subsection{Sybil Detection in Airdrops: Feature-Based Approaches}

Beyond graph structure, feature-based approaches treat Sybil detection as a supervised classification problem.
HasciDB~\cite{hascidb2026} defines five indicators across two axes: operational (BT, BW, HF) and fund-flow (RF, MA).
Models trained on these indicators achieve near-perfect AUC when the label is derived from the same features---a circularity we call the ``rule-recall trap'' and avoid through cross-axis evaluation.
Our companion Paper~1~\cite{wen2026agent} extends the feature space with 8 execution-layer features that distinguish AI agents from humans at $p < 0.01$ (all 8 features), with Cohen's $d$ up to 1.98 for \texttt{hour\_entropy}~\cite{cohen1988}.
These features are designed to capture behavioral invariants---timing regularity, gas-pricing precision, nonce management---that are orthogonal to HasciDB's threshold-based indicators.

\subsection{LLM as Adversary in Security}

The use of LLMs for offensive security tasks is a rapidly growing concern.
Perez et al.~\cite{perez2022redteam} demonstrated that LLMs can automatically generate inputs that elicit harmful outputs from target models, establishing the LLM-as-red-teamer paradigm.
Chao et al.~\cite{chao2023jailbreaking} showed that an attacker LLM can iteratively refine jailbreak prompts, defeating safety alignment in $\le$20 queries via the PAIR algorithm.
Beyond alignment attacks, LLMs have been weaponized against phishing (Heiding et al.~\cite{heiding2024} show that GPT-4-generated phishing emails achieve click rates 1.5$\times$ higher than human-crafted ones), penetration testing (PENTESTGPT~\cite{deng2024llm_pentesting} automates multi-step exploitation), and autonomous web hacking (Fang et al.~\cite{fang2024llm_agents_attack} demonstrate that GPT-4-based agents exploit real-world web vulnerabilities with 73\% success).

In the blockchain Sybil space specifically, LLMhunter~\cite{llmhunter} uses a multi-expert LLM pipeline for Sybil \textit{judgment}---classifying whether a given address is Sybil based on its transaction history.
We invert this direction: rather than using LLMs to \textit{detect} Sybils, we use an LLM to \textit{generate} Sybil-evasive wallet profiles.
This inversion is more than cosmetic.
Detection is a classification problem bounded by the features available; generation is a constrained planning problem that requires the LLM to jointly satisfy multiple detector evasion criteria while maintaining domain plausibility.

\subsection{Positioning This Work}

To our knowledge, this paper is the first to combine: (1)~an LLM adversary that generates adversarial feature vectors, (2)~a multi-detector benchmark spanning four modalities (rules, ML, GBM, graph), (3)~a multi-round adversarial equilibrium analysis with formal convergence criteria, and (4)~a systematic audit for synthetic-feature label leakage.
Prior work attacks one detector at a time; we evaluate cross-modality evasion.
Prior LLM security work operates in natural-language or code domains; we operate on structured numerical features with domain constraints.
The closest analogue is adversarial malware generation~\cite{pierazzi2020}, but malware detectors operate on binaries with rich semantic structure, whereas airdrop detectors operate on low-dimensional indicator vectors---making evasion fundamentally easier and defense fundamentally harder.

\subsection{LLM-Based Sybil Generator}
\label{sec:methods:generator}

We implement the generator using \texttt{claude\_agent\_sdk} with Claude Opus 4.6. The generator:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Reads} HasciDB's 5 indicator definitions and the 8 empirical AI feature distributions.
  \item \textbf{Plans one wallet at a time} under a three-tier sophistication ladder (Table~\ref{tab:tiers}).
  \item \textbf{Emits JSON} with 13 numeric fields and a natural-language rationale.
  \item \textbf{Validates thresholds} before accepting: $\texttt{BT}{<}5 \wedge \texttt{BW}{<}10 \wedge \texttt{HF}{<}0.8 \wedge \texttt{RF}{<}0.5 \wedge \texttt{MA}{<}5$.
  \item \textbf{Caches} all LLM responses by SHA-256 for reproducibility.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent\textbf{Prompt structure.}
The system prompt contains three components: (1)~a \textit{context block} providing HasciDB's indicator definitions, thresholds, and mean/std of each feature from the non-Sybil population; (2)~a \textit{role instruction} directing the LLM to act as a wallet operator planning a single wallet's on-chain footprint; and (3)~a \textit{constraint block} specifying the sophistication tier and output format.
We do not include the full prompt to avoid enabling direct replication for malicious purposes (\S\ref{sec:ethics}), but we release the prompt template with the context block redacted.

\noindent\textbf{Sophistication tiers.}
Table~\ref{tab:tiers} details the three tiers. The key insight is that the difficulty for detectors is \textit{non-monotonic} across tiers: the advanced tier produces internally consistent wallets that are paradoxically easier to detect than moderate-tier wallets on some features, because the LLM's ``too clean'' distributions lack the noise of real human traffic.

\subsection{Cross-Axis Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:methods:crossaxis}

The central methodological move is to \textit{never} train on the same features the label was derived from:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cross-axis LOPO.} Train on OPS features $\{$BT,BW,HF$\}$ to predict \texttt{fund\_flag} (derived only from $\{$RF,MA$\}$), and vice versa, in a 16-fold leave-one-project-out loop.
  \item \textbf{Independent-label transfer.} Train on HasciDB's 5 indicators to predict Gitcoin FDD's independently curated Sybil list.
  \item \textbf{Honest binary classification.} Classify (LLM Sybil) vs (real HasciDB wallet) using only the 8 AI features, eliminating circular labels.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Graph-Based Detection via Louvain Community Detection}
\label{sec:methods:graph}

To evaluate structural defenses, we construct a funding-flow proxy graph $G = (V, E)$ from HasciDB's RF and MA indicators across all 16 projects (up to 8,000 addresses per project).

\noindent\textbf{Graph construction.}
Let $V$ be the set of addresses for a given project.
For each address $v \in V$ with $\text{RF}(v) \ge 0.3$, we create a synthetic funding hub $h_v$ and add edges $(h_v, v)$; addresses sharing a hub form a star topology representing common funding sources.
For each set of addresses $\{v_1, \ldots, v_k\}$ where $\text{MA}(v_i) \ge 3$ for all $i$, we form a clique, representing multi-account clusters.
Non-sybil addresses receive $\text{Poisson}(1.5)$ random edges to model organic transfer activity.
Formally, the edge set is:
\begin{align}
E = E_{\text{RF}} \cup E_{\text{MA}} \cup E_{\text{random}}
\end{align}
where $|E_{\text{RF}}|$ scales linearly with the number of high-RF addresses and $|E_{\text{MA}}|$ scales quadratically with the MA cluster size.
Across 16 projects, the resulting graphs contain 8,000--9,800 nodes and 12,100--21,400 edges (Table~\ref{tab:graph_stats}).

\noindent\textbf{Detection.}
We apply Louvain community detection~\cite{blondel2008louvain} with resolution parameter $\gamma{=}1.0$, which maximizes the modularity $Q = \frac{1}{2m}\sum_{ij}\left[A_{ij} - \gamma\frac{k_ik_j}{2m}\right]\delta(c_i, c_j)$ where $A$ is the adjacency matrix, $k_i$ is the degree of node $i$, $m = |E|$, and $c_i$ is the community assignment.
A community $C$ is flagged as Sybil if $\frac{|\{v \in C : \text{fund\_flag}(v)=1\}|}{|C|} > 0.4$ and $|C| \ge 3$.

This proxy is \textit{conservative}: real graph detectors (SybilRank~\cite{cao2012sybilrank}, SybilBelief~\cite{gong2014sybilbelief}) operate on the full transfer graph with substantially more signal, so our evasion rates may represent upper bounds on what an LLM adversary achieves against production graph detectors.

\subsection{Adversarial Equilibrium Protocol}
\label{sec:methods:equilibrium}

We implement a 10-round Stackelberg game:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Round 0:} Train a GBM detector (150 trees, depth 4, lr 0.1) on 15,000 real wallets + 3,000 LLM sybils at human-blend ratio 0.45.
  \item \textbf{Round $t+1$:} Generate 3,000 new LLM sybils with increased human-blend ratio (0.45$\to$0.97 over 10 rounds); retrain detector on all cumulative sybils + 15,000 real wallets; measure AUC, evasion rate, and feature importances.
  \item \textbf{Convergence criterion:} $|\Delta\text{AUC}|{<}0.01$ for 3 consecutive rounds.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Synthetic-Feature Audit Protocol}
\label{sec:methods:audit}

Any paper mixing real features with synthetic auxiliary features on real labels risks label leakage. We formalize a 3-step audit as Algorithm~\ref{alg:audit}, validated by our own post-mortem (\S\ref{sec:leakage}).

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Synthetic-Feature Audit Checklist}
\label{alg:audit}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Dataset $\mathcal{D}$ with real features $\mathbf{X}_r$, synthetic features $\mathbf{X}_s$, label $y$
\STATE \textbf{Step 1 (Independence).} Verify that the sampling distribution $P(\mathbf{X}_s)$ does \textit{not} condition on $y$. If $\mathbf{X}_s \sim P(\cdot \mid y)$, the pipeline leaks labels.
\STATE \textbf{Step 2 (Auxiliary-only test).} Train a classifier on $\mathbf{X}_s$ alone to predict $y$. If AUC $\gg 0.5$, the synthetic features contain label information.
\STATE \textbf{Step 3 (Leakage-free ablation).} Redefine the task to avoid the circular label (e.g., classify LLM sybils vs.\ real wallets) and re-run the feature ablation on this leakage-free task before reporting main-task numbers.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Independent-Label Transfer (HasciDB $\to$ Gitcoin FDD)}
\label{sec:fdd}

As an external validation of detector generalization, we test whether HasciDB's features transfer to an independently curated label source.
On the gitcoin subset ($n{=}23{,}878$): HasciDB flags 9,991 (41.8\%), while Gitcoin's Fraud Detection \& Defense (FDD) team independently flags only 140 (0.59\%); the Jaccard overlap between the two label sets is 0.006.
This near-zero overlap is itself informative: the two methodologies operationalize ``Sybil'' differently. HasciDB flags behavioral automation patterns; FDD flags grant-farming and collusion.

A GBM trained on HasciDB's 5 indicators to predict FDD Sybil labels yields 5-fold CV AUC 0.586---above chance ($p < 0.01$ by permutation test) but far below 1.0.
This result has two implications.
First, it confirms that HasciDB's features contain \textit{some} signal about independently identified Sybils, validating the behavioral indicators as partial proxies for real-world Sybil activity.
Second, it establishes that the two label sources capture overlapping but non-identical Sybil populations, motivating the need for multi-source detection pipelines that combine behavioral, financial, and social signals.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Cross-Modality Threat Assessment}

The central finding is that a single LLM adversary defeats every deployed detector class simultaneously (Table~\ref{tab:graph_evasion}). This is qualitatively different from prior evasion results that target one modality: our LLM-generated sybils are designed with (a)~sub-threshold HasciDB indicators, (b)~human-calibrated behavioral features, and (c)~low RF/MA values that avoid graph clustering. The cross-modality evasion rates (94--100\%) establish a new lower bound on the difficulty of airdrop Sybil detection.

\subsection{What Signal Survives}

\texttt{behavioral\_consistency} emerges as the single most robust feature: top-1 in all 10 adversarial rounds and individually the highest-AUC single feature on moderate-tier adversaries (0.762). \texttt{hour\_entropy} is strongest on basic/advanced tiers (0.864--0.878) but vulnerable to targeted evasion on moderate. The top-3 features together (behavioral\_consistency + hour\_entropy + response\_latency\_variance) reach $\ge$0.87 AUC across all tiers, suggesting a lightweight production detector.

\subsection{The Cross-Axis Floor}

Cross-axis LOPO mean AUC 0.617 is our honest floor for HasciDB. Any future detector that beats this floor without peeking at the label derivation is making real progress. Any detector reporting AUC $>$0.95 on HasciDB's \texttt{is\_sybil} with the 5 indicators as features is measuring rule recall, not generalization.

\subsection{Implications for Airdrop Design}

Our results carry concrete implications for how protocol designers should structure eligibility criteria and anti-Sybil defenses.

\noindent\textbf{1. Publish labels, not rules.}
HasciDB's primary value is the labeled dataset, not the detection thresholds.
The moment thresholds are published, an LLM adversary can read them and plan below them (100\% evasion by construction).
We recommend that future airdrop audits publish labeled address sets (with provenance metadata) but keep the detection logic private until after distribution.
This follows the principle from Carlini \& Wagner~\cite{carlini2017}: security-through-obscurity is fragile, but forcing the attacker to guess the exact decision boundary does raise the bar.

\noindent\textbf{2. Invest in execution-layer behavioral signals.}
Gas precision, nonce regularity, and hour entropy are cheap to compute (requiring only the transaction history from any block explorer API) and do not require off-chain identity.
Our ablation shows that top-3 behavioral features alone achieve AUC $\ge 0.87$ across all adversary tiers---a strong starting point for production deployment.
Protocol teams should compute these features for all eligible addresses and flag outliers for manual review, even if they pass rule-based checks.

\noindent\textbf{3. Treat detection as adversarial from day one.}
Pre-airdrop detection achieves AUC 0.793 on non-adversarial wallets~\cite{wen2026preairdrop} but collapses to 0\% detection under LLM attack.
Any production deployment must include adversarial training: generate synthetic adversarial wallets using the methodology in \S\ref{sec:methods:generator}, augment the training set, and retrain.
Our equilibrium analysis (\S\ref{sec:equilibrium}) shows that even a single round of adversarial augmentation raises detection AUC from 0.0 (rule-based) to 0.985, and three rounds stabilize it above 0.70.

\noindent\textbf{4. Combine modalities.}
No single detector class survives the LLM adversary: rules collapse by construction, ML detectors trained on the same features are circular, and graph detectors miss wallets with low fund-flow footprints.
A layered defense combining behavioral features (execution-layer), structural features (graph topology), and temporal features (activity cadence) is necessary.
The key insight is that these modalities are \textit{complementary}: behavioral features catch LLM sybils that evade graph detectors (because they lack funding clusters), while graph detectors catch multi-account clusters that evade behavioral features (because they may exhibit human-like timing).

\subsection{Toward Robust Multi-Modal Detection}

The cross-modality evasion result (Table~\ref{tab:graph_evasion}) suggests that no single-modality detector is sufficient.
We sketch a multi-modal defense architecture that would combine the strengths of each approach.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 1: Behavioral gating.}
Apply the top-3 AI feature detector (hour\_entropy, behavioral\_consistency, response\_latency\_variance) as a first-pass filter.
Addresses scoring above a conservative threshold ($\ge 0.8$ predicted probability) are flagged.
This layer catches LLM sybils with AUC $\ge 0.87$ and runs in $O(n)$ time per address.

\noindent\textbf{Layer 2: Graph clustering.}
For addresses passing Layer~1, construct a transfer graph and run Louvain community detection.
Flag communities with sybil-indicator fraction $> 0.4$.
This layer catches multi-account clusters with precision 0.75 (Table~\ref{tab:graph_stats}).

\noindent\textbf{Layer 3: Temporal anomaly detection.}
Compute temporal features (burst frequency, inter-transaction time distribution, day-of-week entropy) and flag addresses with anomalously regular patterns.
This layer addresses the ``too clean'' signature of advanced LLM sybils.

\noindent\textbf{Ensemble.}
Combine the three layers via a calibrated ensemble (e.g., stacking with a logistic regression meta-learner).
The key assumption is that an adversary that evades all three layers simultaneously would need to: (a)~match human behavioral distributions across 8 features, (b)~avoid funding-flow clustering, and (c)~exhibit realistically irregular temporal patterns---a conjunction of constraints that substantially raises the attack cost.

\subsection{Stackelberg Equilibrium and Formal Game-Theoretic Framing}

The 10-round adversarial equilibrium can be formalized as a Stackelberg game~\cite{bruckner2011stackelberg,osborne1994game} between a defender (leader) and an attacker (follower):

\begin{align}
\min_{\theta} \max_{\phi} \; & \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\phi} \left[ \mathbf{1}[f_\theta(x) < \tau] \right] \label{eq:stackelberg} \\
\text{s.t.} \quad & x_i \in [\ell_i, u_i] \; \forall i \in \{1, \ldots, 13\} \nonumber \\
& \text{BT}(x) < 5, \; \text{BW}(x) < 10, \; \text{HF}(x) < 0.8 \nonumber
\end{align}

\noindent where $f_\theta$ is the detector parameterized by $\theta$, $p_\phi$ is the LLM-generated sybil distribution parameterized by $\phi$ (the human-blend ratio), $\tau$ is the detection threshold, and $[\ell_i, u_i]$ are the domain constraints on each feature.

The defender moves first by choosing $\theta$ (training the detector); the attacker observes $\theta$ and responds by choosing $\phi$ to maximize evasion.
In our protocol, the attacker's strategy space is parameterized by the human-blend ratio (0.45 to 0.97), which controls how closely the generated features match the human distribution.

The convergence to AUC $\approx 0.60$ with $\Delta\text{AUC} < 0.01$ at round~9 suggests an approximate equilibrium.
At this point, the defender cannot improve without access to new feature modalities (the attacker has exhausted the blend-ratio axis), and the attacker cannot further degrade detection without violating domain constraints (blend ratio is already at 0.97---very close to the human distribution).

The equilibrium AUC of 0.60 is above chance (0.50) because \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} encodes a structural invariant---the coefficient of variation of inter-arrival times---that an LLM cannot perfectly replicate without real-time traffic modeling.
This residual signal represents the \textit{cost of mimicry}: even a maximally sophisticated LLM adversary leaves a detectable footprint in the temporal structure of transactions.

\subsection{Ethical Considerations}
\label{sec:ethics}

This work raises dual-use concerns that we address directly.

\noindent\textbf{Responsible disclosure.}
We shared our evasion results with the HasciDB team prior to public release (March 2026), giving them 60 days to incorporate adversarial training into their pipeline.
We do not release the full LLM prompt used for sybil generation; we release only the prompt template with the distribution-calibration context block redacted.
The cached LLM responses (JSON feature vectors) are released for reproducibility, as they do not contain actionable attack code---they are numerical vectors that require the full HasciDB pipeline to be useful.

\noindent\textbf{Attacker advantage is already public.}
The key ingredients for the attack---HasciDB's thresholds, indicator definitions, and the general idea of LLM-assisted blockchain interaction---are already publicly available.
Our contribution is not to enable the attack but to \textit{measure its effectiveness} and \textit{develop defenses}.
Without rigorous evaluation, protocol teams would continue deploying detectors with a false sense of security.

\noindent\textbf{Defensive value exceeds offensive risk.}
The behavioral defense (AUC 0.987) and the adversarial training protocol (\S\ref{sec:equilibrium}) are the primary deliverables of this work.
A protocol team that reads this paper and deploys the top-3 feature detector with adversarial training will be substantially better defended than one that relies on HasciDB rules alone.

\noindent\textbf{Broader implications.}
The LLM-as-adversary paradigm extends beyond airdrops.
Any system where detection relies on behavioral heuristics with published thresholds is vulnerable to the same attack.
Anti-fraud systems, spam filters, and bot detectors all face analogous threats as LLMs become capable of planning within numerical constraint spaces.
We urge the security community to adopt adversarial evaluation as a standard practice for any threshold-based detection system.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

We identify seven limitations that bound the generalizability of our results, and discuss what findings would change under relaxed assumptions.

\noindent\textbf{1. Feature-space attack, not problem-space.}
The LLM outputs a JSON feature vector, not real on-chain transactions.
While our pilot (\S\ref{sec:problemspace}) shows $<$8.3\% relative gap between planned and simulated feature values, a full problem-space attack would require: funding each wallet from a unique source (to avoid RF clustering), spacing transactions realistically (to match \texttt{hour\_entropy} targets), and paying real gas costs.
The gas cost constraint is non-trivial: at current Ethereum L1 gas prices ($\sim$30 Gwei, April 2026), generating a realistic 200-transaction history costs approximately 0.1--0.5 ETH per wallet ($\sim$\$200--\$1,000), which may be economical for high-value airdrops (Arbitrum distributed \$1.2B) but prohibitive at scale for smaller campaigns.
A full testnet deployment with real HasciDB recomputation remains the critical next step.

\noindent\textbf{2. Proxy graph, not full transfer graph.}
Our Louvain evaluation uses a funding-flow proxy graph constructed from HasciDB's RF and MA indicators, not the raw Ethereum transfer graph.
The proxy graph contains 8,000--10,000 nodes and 12,000--21,000 edges per project, whereas the full transfer graph may have $10\times$--$100\times$ more edges (including all ERC-20 transfers, DEX swaps, and bridge transactions).
Real graph detectors---SybilRank~\cite{cao2012sybilrank}, SybilBelief~\cite{gong2014sybilbelief}, and GNN-based approaches~\cite{artemis}---operate on this richer graph and likely achieve higher recall on real Sybils.
Consequently, our LLM evasion rates (94--100\%) should be interpreted as upper bounds; a full-graph evaluation may reveal structural signatures that the LLM cannot plan around without actually executing transactions.

\noindent\textbf{3. Single chain (Ethereum L1 only).}
All 16 projects in our evaluation are Ethereum L1 airdrops.
L2 rollups (Arbitrum, Optimism, Base, zkSync) differ in three ways that affect AI feature transferability: (a)~gas mechanics are fundamentally different (L2 gas is 10--100$\times$ cheaper, potentially masking gas-precision signals), (b)~transaction throughput is higher (enabling more realistic burst behavior without high cost), and (c)~sequencer ordering introduces different timing characteristics that may alter \texttt{hour\_entropy} distributions.
We hypothesize that behavioral features remain informative on L2 but that their relative importance and thresholds would shift; an L2 replication study is needed.

\noindent\textbf{4. Single LLM model (Claude Opus 4.6 only).}
Our adversary uses a single commercial LLM.
Different models (GPT-4, Gemini Ultra, open-source alternatives) may produce different evasion profiles.
In particular, open-source models can be fine-tuned specifically for Sybil generation, potentially achieving higher evasion rates on behavioral features.
Conversely, smaller models may lack the planning capacity to jointly satisfy all 13 feature constraints, resulting in lower evasion on the advanced tier.
A multi-model adversary benchmark is needed to establish the robustness of our findings across LLM families.

\noindent\textbf{5. Convergence not formally reached.}
The adversarial equilibrium achieves $\Delta\text{AUC} = 0.009$ at round~9 but does not reach 3 consecutive rounds below 0.01 (our pre-registered convergence criterion).
The mean $\Delta$AUC over the last 3 rounds is 0.012, suggesting convergence within 1--2 additional rounds.
The equilibrium AUC of $\approx$0.60 should therefore be treated as an estimate; the true equilibrium may be slightly lower (0.58--0.60).

\noindent\textbf{6. 4-project leakage-fix subset.}
The label-leakage analysis (\S\ref{sec:leakage}) uses a 4-project subset (1inch, uniswap, ens, blur\_s2).
The full 16-project honest rerun is pending; we expect qualitatively identical results because the leakage mechanism (label-conditional sampling in \texttt{augment\_with\_ai\_features}) is project-independent.

\noindent\textbf{7. What would change our conclusions.}
Our central claim---that LLM adversaries defeat all deployed detector modalities---would be falsified if: (a)~a full-graph detector achieves $\ge$50\% recall on LLM sybils (indicating that graph structure captures signals invisible to indicators); (b)~problem-space deployment reveals that maintaining \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} $< 0.2$ is infeasible at scale (due to gas-cost or timing constraints); or (c)~a multi-modal detector combining behavioral + graph + temporal features achieves AUC $\ge 0.95$ on the adversarial equilibrium task.
Any of these would narrow (though not eliminate) the threat we document.

\noindent\textbf{Future work.}
We identify five directions:
(a)~Closed-loop adversarial training where the LLM generator receives reward signals from the defender, enabling RL-style policy optimization rather than static blend-ratio scheduling.
(b)~Problem-space attacks on an Ethereum testnet with full HasciDB recomputation, measuring the actual gas cost of evasion.
(c)~Cross-method transfer at scale using $\ge$3 independent label sources (HasciDB, Gitcoin FDD, Hop Protocol's internal Sybil list) to establish detection floors that are robust to label methodology.
(d)~L2 replication across Arbitrum, Optimism, and Base, testing whether behavioral features transfer across gas-pricing regimes.
(e)~A joint feature+graph adversary that simultaneously plans both the behavioral profile and the funding structure of each wallet, testing whether the multi-modal defense architecture sketched in \S\ref{sec:discussion} is robust to coordinated attacks.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We asked whether airdrop Sybil detection survives an LLM adversary. The answer is nuanced. \textbf{No single detector modality survives:} LLM-generated sybils evade rules (100\%), ML (100\%), GBM (99.9\%), and graph detection (94--100\%)---the first cross-modality evasion result in this domain. \textbf{But behavioral fingerprinting works:} an 8-feature execution-layer detector achieves AUC 0.987 on the hardest adversary tier, with \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} as the most robust single feature. \textbf{The arms race converges:} a 10-round Stackelberg game drives AUC from 0.985 to 0.599, with \texttt{behavioral\_consistency} ranking top-1 in all rounds. Along the way, we documented a label-leakage bug (AUC 0.953$\to$0.609), formalized a 3-step audit protocol (Algorithm~\ref{alg:audit}), and released all artifacts for reproducibility. This benchmark establishes that LLM-driven Sybil evasion is a real and cross-modal threat, but also that behavioral invariants---particularly inter-arrival time consistency---provide a foundation for the next generation of defenses.
