\begin{abstract}
Existing benchmarks for detecting automated activity on public blockchains treat label quality as an afterthought: addresses are labeled by the same behavioral heuristics that serve as classifier features, producing apparent AUCs above~0.95 that vanish once label leakage is controlled. We release \textbf{OnChainAgentID}, a provenance-verified benchmark of 1{,}147 Ethereum addresses (533 agents, 614 humans) drawn from 18 operational categories whose labels are anchored to non-behavioral provenance (on-chain registries, relay-attested MEV bots, ENS registration events, Gitcoin donations, PoolTogether deposits, etc.). The benchmark defines a fixed \emph{five-level evaluation protocol}---in-sample fit, random 10-fold CV, temporal holdout, a 70-address strict curated core, and leave-one-platform-out---that exposes three distinct failure modes: label--feature entanglement, temporal drift, and cross-platform non-transfer. We ship a Gebru-style datasheet, frozen train/test splits for every level, a \texttt{pip}-installable audit harness (\texttt{onchain\_audit}) that runs a three-step leakage check on any user-supplied classifier, and baseline results for four tabular models and a graph attention network. On our baselines, a single Random Forest on the same 1{,}147-address dataset yields AUC 0.84 (in-sample on heuristic labels), 0.80 (random 10-fold), 0.67 (temporal holdout), 0.76 (strict core), and 0.40 (single-class LOPO) / 0.74 (mixed-class LOPO)---all numbers use the same model and dataset for direct comparability, and the progression is one we argue \emph{every} submission to this benchmark should report. The benchmark is released under CC~BY~4.0 with a Zenodo DOI; the audit harness is MIT-licensed. Our goal is to give blockchain-ML researchers a shared, leakage-resistant evaluation substrate so that future agent-detection claims can be compared on equal footing and audited for free.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

\paragraph{The gap.}
Autonomous AI agents now manage billions of dollars on Ethereum. Platforms such as Autonolas, Fetch.ai, AI Arena, and Virtuals Protocol make deployment turnkey, and a growing security literature treats ``AI-controlled address'' as a first-class category. From the protocol's perspective, however, an agent-driven externally-owned account (EOA) is indistinguishable from a human wallet---no public registry covers agents exhaustively, and no chain-native signal separates automated from manual activity. Reliable on-chain agent detection would enable agent-aware protocol design, security auditing, and regulatory monitoring. But \emph{reliable detection requires a reliable benchmark}, and the current empirical practice in the field has a structural problem.

\paragraph{The problem: label leakage is the norm, not the exception.}
Published blockchain bot-detection results rely on labels produced by behavioral heuristics (e.g., ``more than $N$ transactions per week,'' ``$>1$~bit hourly entropy,'' ``inter-transaction coefficient of variation $<1$''). The same behavioral quantities then appear, with renaming or an algebraic transform, as classifier features. Resulting models achieve AUC~$>0.95$ not because they detect agents but because they re-derive the labeling rule. In our own prior work on an agent-labeled corpus of 3{,}316 addresses, removing three leaked features (\texttt{active\_hour\_entropy}, \texttt{burst\_frequency}, \texttt{tx\_interval\_std}) dropped gradient-boosted AUC from 0.98 to 0.80. To our knowledge, no existing blockchain classification benchmark ships with a leakage-audit protocol attached.

\paragraph{What we release.}
\textbf{OnChainAgentID} packages four artifacts that together let any researcher run a leakage-resistant agent-detection evaluation end-to-end:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A dataset} of 1{,}147 Ethereum addresses with 23 behavioral features and provenance-verified binary labels, plus per-address raw transaction histories (4{,}854 files total across mining pool). Addresses come from 18 operational categories, 9 agent and 9 human, and every label is traceable to a non-behavioral provenance event (registry enrollment, relay attestation, ENS action, or hand curation). Fully documented with a Gebru et al.\ (2018)-style datasheet.
    \item \textbf{A fixed five-level evaluation protocol} with frozen JSON splits. Levels range from a sanity-check in-sample fit (Level~1) through random 10-fold CV (Level~2), temporal holdout (Level~3), a 70-address strict curated core with no heuristic labels (Level~4), to leave-one-platform-out over 14 platform folds (Level~5). The five levels probe three orthogonal failure modes (label entanglement, temporal drift, cross-platform non-transfer), and we argue submissions should always report all five.
    \item \textbf{A \texttt{pip}-installable audit harness}, \texttt{onchain\_audit}, that runs a three-step leakage check (label--feature overlap, label-purity tier comparison, cross-scheme transfer) on any user-supplied classifier and emits a Markdown verdict report. The harness is designed to be applied both to this benchmark and to new datasets the user brings.
    \item \textbf{Baseline results} for four tabular classifiers (Random Forest, Gradient Boosting, Logistic Regression, LightGBM) and one graph neural network (GAT) at every level, together with a per-level leaderboard template for future submissions.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{What a user of the benchmark does.}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \texttt{pip install onchain-audit}; clone the benchmark repo (frozen Zenodo DOI).
    \item Load \texttt{features\_provenance\_v4.parquet} and \texttt{labels\_provenance\_v4.json} plus the five split JSONs.
    \item Train their classifier on each split and report AUC/F1/precision/recall on all five levels.
    \item Run \texttt{generate\_audit\_report(clf, dataset)} to verify no leakage indicator triggers.
    \item Submit their numbers to the leaderboard template (Table~\ref{tab:leaderboard}) along with a short methods note and the audit report.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{What this paper is not.}
It is not a new agent-detection \emph{method}. The five-level collapse from 0.84 to 0.40/0.74 on our own pipeline is included as a self-audit case study (\S\ref{sec:audit}) demonstrating how to apply the toolkit, not as a methodological proposal. The contributions here are the dataset, the evaluation protocol, the harness, and the baselines---artifacts, not algorithms.

\section{Related Datasets}
\label{sec:related}

The \emph{Elliptic}~\cite{weber2019antimoney} Bitcoin AML dataset uses coarse \texttt{licit}/\texttt{illicit}/\texttt{unknown} labels and ships no fixed cross-platform split; results are known to be sensitive to graph re-splitting. \emph{Ethereum phishing} datasets~\cite{patel2021phishing} mix user reports with behavioral rules; Tikhomirov and Ivanitskiy~\cite{tikhomirov2021phishing} specifically warned that heuristic phishing labels leak address-type features. Victor et al.~\cite{victor2023fingerprinting} release MEV-bot behavioral data but no human counterpart, so cross-class distinctions are confounded with class imbalance. Li et al.~\cite{li2024telegram} cover a single bot family. Social-media bot benchmarks~\cite{cresci2017paradigm,varol2017online} report 0.75--0.85 AUC under mixed-source labels, which mask label provenance post-hoc.

Three structural gaps motivated a new benchmark rather than reuse: (i)~existing datasets do not publish their labeling rules in machine-checkable form, so label--feature overlap cannot be audited; (ii)~published blockchain benchmarks ship at most random-CV splits, so a high number cannot be distinguished from platform fingerprinting; (iii)~no public dataset provides a provenance-verified agent-vs-human binary with both classes drawn from $\geq 7$ independent sources. Recent surveys~\cite{he2026agentsurvey,shamsi2023gnnsurvey} catalog agent platforms and GNN-on-blockchain methods but do not release benchmarks; Wang et al.~\cite{wang2024airisks} audit AI-agent wallet risks at the prompt-injection level, orthogonal to address classification. OnChainAgentID sits below these as a shared evaluation substrate.

\section{Dataset Description}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{Instances and fields}

Each of the 1{,}147 instances is a single Ethereum address together with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item 23 numerical behavioral features, grouped as \emph{temporal} (7), \emph{gas} (6), \emph{interaction} (5), and \emph{approval/security} (5). See \S\ref{sec:features} and Appendix~\ref{app:features} for the full list.
    \item A binary \texttt{label} ($1 = \text{agent}$, $0 = \text{human}$).
    \item Provenance metadata: \texttt{provenance\_source}, \texttt{label\_provenance}~$\in\{0,1\}$, and \texttt{category}.
    \item Raw Etherscan transaction history (blockNumber, timeStamp, from, to, value, gas, gasPrice, gasUsed, isError, methodId, \ldots), up to 10{,}000 most recent transactions per address, released separately as one parquet per address.
\end{itemize}

\texttt{label\_provenance}~$=1$ indicates an off-chain or registry-level attestation (Flashbots relay, Etherscan label, Arkham label, ENS verification, hand curation); \texttt{label\_provenance}~$=0$ indicates a strictly on-chain heuristic such as ``called \texttt{performUpkeep} on Chainlink Registry $\geq 3$ times.'' Both are traceable to non-behavioral events, in contrast to labels gated on 23-feature-level statistics.

\subsection{Collection process}

\textbf{Tier 1---curation (70 addresses).} The strict curated core was seeded from Flashbots relay attestations, Etherscan agent tags (jaredfromsubway, Wintermute, Autonolas service operators), and Arkham entity labels for agents; and from ENS verification (e.g., vitalik.eth), Twitter-verified ENS resolvers, and DAO governance voters for humans. No address in the curated core passes through any behavioral threshold.

\textbf{Tier 2---on-chain heuristic mining ($\sim$1{,}077 addresses).} For each platform (Chainlink, Compound V3, Gelato, Keep3r, Gitcoin, PoolTogether, ENS) we wrote a SQL-like query against the Etherscan V2 API that matches the platform's canonical interaction pattern (e.g., ``called \texttt{performUpkeep} on \texttt{0x\ldots} $\geq 3$ times in 30 days''). These heuristics are \emph{provenance}-level: they check for interaction with a specific registry contract, not for statistical properties of the address's behavior.

\textbf{Feature extraction.} We fetch up to 10{,}000 historical transactions per address from Etherscan and compute the 23 behavioral features via \texttt{features/feature\_pipeline.py}. NaN values are imputed at the column median and 1st/99th-percentile clipped at train time (not in the stored parquet).

Mining window: 2026-04-07 to 2026-04-09. Transactions span block 18{,}000{,}000 to block 22{,}000{,}000 (late 2023 through April 2026). No human subjects were involved; no personal data is included except where subjects already self-attested publicly.

\subsection{Feature groups}
\label{sec:features}

The 23 features split into four groups: \emph{temporal} (7: inter-transaction interval moments, hour-of-day entropy, burst frequency), \emph{gas} (6: gas-price precision statistics, EIP-1559 tip precision, gas--nonce correlation), \emph{interaction} (5: unique contract ratio, Herfindahl concentration, method-ID diversity, contract-to-EOA ratio, n-gram repetition), and \emph{approval/security} (5: unlimited-approve ratio, revoke ratio, unverified-contract approvals, multi-protocol count, flash-loan flag). Full definitions in Appendix~\ref{app:features}.

\subsection{Datasheet summary}

A full Gebru et al.~\cite{gebru2018datasheets} datasheet ships at \texttt{benchmark/datasheet.md}. Headline points: (i)~\emph{motivation}---give blockchain-ML researchers a leakage-auditable substrate; (ii)~\emph{composition}---1{,}147 primary, 70 strict core, 4{,}854 raw-tx files; both classes from $\geq 7$ provenance sources; (iii)~\emph{collection}---Etherscan V2 API, no crowdsourcing, no IRB; (iv)~\emph{uses}---benchmarking under the five-level protocol and auditing user classifiers; \emph{not for} deanonymization, production fraud detection, or LLM-specific agent detection (see \S\ref{sec:limitations}); (v)~\emph{license}---CC~BY~4.0 data, MIT code; (vi)~\emph{known biases}---platform imbalance (\texttt{defi\_hf\_trader}~=~17\%), survivor bias ($\geq 10$ tx), time-window bias (no pre-2023-11 addresses).

\section{Evaluation Protocol: The Five-Level Benchmark}
\label{sec:protocol}

The core methodological contribution of OnChainAgentID is a \emph{fixed} five-level evaluation protocol that every submission should report. Each level probes a distinct failure mode; the gap between levels quantifies the magnitude of the exposed failure. All splits are shipped as frozen JSON in \texttt{benchmark/splits/}.

\paragraph{Level 1 (in-sample fit / leaky-label reference).} RF 10$\times$10-fold CV on 1{,}129 of the 1{,}147 addresses (18 could not be assigned C1--C4 labels) relabeled with the heuristic C1--C4 oracle (860 agents, 269 humans). AUC~0.844$\pm$0.052. This number is \emph{not} a sanity check on model capacity; it quantifies the inflated ceiling that leaky labels produce with the same model and features used at every other level.

\paragraph{Level 2 (random stratified 10-fold CV).} \texttt{level2\_random\_10fold.json}: 10 stratified folds, seed~42, $\sim$115 test addresses each. \emph{Report:} mean $\pm$ std of AUC, F1, precision, recall. This is an upper bound under within-distribution assumptions.

\paragraph{Level 3 (temporal holdout).} \texttt{level3\_temporal\_holdout.json}: split at the median first-seen block (block~12{,}321{,}026 $\approx$ 2021-04-27). Train: 573 addresses (170 agents, 403 humans; 70\% human). Test: 574 (363 agents, 211 humans; 63\% agent). The class-distribution shift mirrors the post-2021 proliferation of agent platforms; the gap to Level~2 quantifies temporal drift.

\paragraph{Level 4 (strict curated core).} \texttt{level4\_strict\_core.json}: 70 hand-curated addresses (35 agents, 35 humans) drawn from \texttt{pilot\_agent}, \texttt{curated\_mev\_bot}, \texttt{expanded\_mev\_bot}, \texttt{curated\_human}, \texttt{pilot\_human}, \texttt{expanded\_human}. Evaluate via LOO-CV or repeated stratified 5-fold. \emph{Report:} LOO AUC and bootstrap CI95. No label here passes through a behavioral threshold, so Level~4 is the honest ceiling. (N=70; earlier drafts incorrectly reported N=64.)

\paragraph{Level 5 (leave-one-platform-out, LOPO).} \texttt{level5\_lopo.json}: 14 folds, one per platform category with $\geq 10$ addresses. In fold $i$, all addresses from platform $i$ are held out; the remaining 13 form the train set. \emph{Report:} per-platform accuracy (= per-class recall, since each platform is single-class) plus pooled AUC over the union of held-out predictions.

\paragraph{Structural caveat on Level 5.} The single-class-per-platform design conflates domain shift with concept failure, so Level~5 measures \emph{cross-context deployment readiness} rather than pure concept generalization. The true cross-concept gap lies between Level~2 and Level~5; v5 of the benchmark will add mixed-class platforms to separate the two.

\section{Audit Toolkit}
\label{sec:audit}

\subsection{Installation and use}

The \texttt{onchain\_audit} Python package accompanies the benchmark. A three-line end-to-end audit is:

\begin{verbatim}
pip install onchain-audit
from onchain_audit import generate_audit_report, load_example_dataset
print(generate_audit_report(my_clf, load_example_dataset()))
\end{verbatim}

The output is a Markdown report (writable to disk, pasteable into an appendix) with a \textbf{PASS}/\textbf{FAIL} verdict followed by three diagnostic sections: label--feature overlap, label-purity tier comparison, and cross-scheme transfer. On a commodity laptop, the full audit runs in $\sim$1~minute.

\subsection{The three-step protocol}

\textbf{Step 1---Label--feature overlap.} Given the mining rules used to produce the labels and the classifier features, compute (a) Jaccard similarity between the boolean support of each rule and the median-binarized support of each feature and (b) Pearson and Spearman correlation between each rule and each feature. Risk flag if any pair exceeds $J \geq 0.5$ or $|\rho| \geq 0.6$.

\textbf{Step 2---Label-purity tier comparison.} Evaluate the classifier independently on each label-purity tier. For OnChainAgentID, the default tiers are \texttt{all\_mined} (both heuristic and curated labels), \texttt{curated\_only} (\texttt{label\_provenance}~$=1$), and \texttt{strict\_core} (the 70-address Level~4 set). A drop in AUC of $\geq 0.15$ between the best and worst tier indicates the classifier's lift depends on label purity.

\textbf{Step 3---Cross-scheme transfer.} Partition the addresses into two schemes whose labels come from independent provenance sources (e.g., on-chain registry labels vs.\ Flashbots/Arkham attestations). Train on scheme~A, evaluate on scheme~B, and vice versa. A transfer gap $\geq 0.15$ below each scheme's internal CV suggests the classifier learned scheme-specific cues rather than a stable agent-vs-human signal.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:audit} formalizes the full procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\small
\SetAlgoLined
\KwIn{Features $\mathcal{F}$, labels $y$, mining rules $\mathcal{R}$, classifier $h$, purity tiers $\{T_1,\ldots,T_k\}$, schemes $(A, B)$}
\KwOut{Verdict $\in\{\textsc{Pass}, \textsc{Fail}\}$ plus a diagnostic report}

\textbf{Step 1:} For each $(r,f) \in \mathcal{R}\times\mathcal{F}$, compute $J(r,f)$ and $|\text{corr}(r,f)|$; flag any pair above threshold.\;
\textbf{Step 2:} Train $h$ independently on each tier $T_i$ under 5$\times$3 repeated stratified CV; record $a_i$. Warn if $\max_i a_i - \min_i a_i \geq 0.15$.\;
\textbf{Step 3:} Compute internal CV AUC $a_A, a_B$; cross-train $h$ on $A$, test on $B$, and vice versa, giving $t_{A\to B}, t_{B\to A}$. Warn if $\mathrm{mean}(a_A,a_B) - \mathrm{mean}(t_{A\to B}, t_{B\to A}) \geq 0.15$.\;
\textbf{Verdict:} \textsc{Fail} if any step warns; \textsc{Pass} otherwise.\;
\caption{\texttt{generate\_audit\_report}: three-step leakage audit.}
\label{alg:audit}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Self-audit case study (why the harness exists)}

We applied the harness to our own prior 3{,}316-address pipeline, which used a 4-gate labeling oracle (C1--C4) that gated AGENT labels on \texttt{hour\_entropy~>~2.5}, \texttt{burst\_ratio~>~0.1}, and \texttt{tx\_interval\_cv~<~1.0}. Three of the top-four classifier features (\texttt{active\_hour\_entropy}, \texttt{burst\_frequency}, \texttt{tx\_interval\_std}) are algebraically identical or near-identical to the three gate variables. The resulting GBM achieved AUC~0.98, a number that made its way into an earlier draft as the headline.

Running the harness produced the following (abbreviated):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Step 1 flag}: three rules have $|\rho| > 0.9$ with features bearing the same name up to renaming.
    \item \textbf{Step 2 flag}: AUC on \texttt{all\_mined} is 0.98; on \texttt{strict\_core} (70 addresses), 0.63. Drop of 0.35 (threshold 0.15).
    \item \textbf{Step 3 flag}: training on C1--C4 Autonolas holders and testing on curated MEV bots yields AUC~0.27 (below chance); the reverse transfer yields~0.34.
\end{itemize}
All three indicators triggered, so the harness verdict was \textbf{FAIL}. The fix was to demote C1--C4 to inference-only and build the provenance-verified v4 set that OnChainAgentID now ships. The harness is designed to catch exactly this failure mode for future users \emph{before} the high AUC number becomes a headline. \S\ref{sec:baselines} reports the post-fix numbers that Table~\ref{tab:leaderboard} uses as baselines.

\subsection{Level 5: Leave-One-Platform-Out}

The original single-class LOPO yields pooled RF AUC 0.40 (0.397), but this conflates domain shift with concept failure. A mixed-class LOPO---holding out behavioral clusters (K-Means $k{=}5$) that contain both agents and humans---recovers AUC to \textbf{0.74} (RF) / 0.75 (GBM) ($+$33\,pp). The cascade floor is therefore 0.74, not 0.40. The remaining gap to random CV (0.80) represents genuine cross-cluster heterogeneity, but the classifier retains strong discrimination when held-out folds contain both classes.

Pooled over 14 held-out platform folds (1{,}131 predictions), RF reaches pooled AUC~0.397 and pooled accuracy~47.0\%. Per-platform recalls range from 20\% (\texttt{defi\_hf\_trader}) to 87\% (\texttt{pilot\_agent}); human specificities range from 46\% (\texttt{human\_ens\_interaction}) to 79\% (\texttt{human\_exchange\_depositor}). The pooled AUC below 0.50 confirms that, under the single-class-per-platform LOPO, current features do not transfer across platforms.

\section{Ethics and Datasheet Summary}
\label{sec:ethics}

All 1{,}147 addresses are pseudonymous public blockchain identifiers; no personal data is attached. The handful with a real-world identity (e.g., vitalik.eth, Wintermute) are publicly self-attested. No IRB required. \emph{Misuses and mitigations:} (i)~\emph{deanonymization}---the benchmark tags only behavioral archetypes, never identities not already public; (ii)~\emph{adversarial evasion}---feature importances could inform evasion, an acceptable open-science cost; (iii)~\emph{over-reliance on Level~2}---mitigated by the five-level reporting convention, the audit harness, and the leaderboard template. \emph{Positive uses:} agent-aware protocol design, security auditing of agent-held DeFi positions, regulatory monitoring within known distributions, and as a teaching artifact. \emph{License:} CC~BY~4.0 (data), MIT (code); Zenodo DOI at camera-ready; erratum policy anchored on dated \texttt{FINDINGS\_YYYY-MM-DD.md}.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{sec:limitations}

\emph{Chain coverage.} Ethereum mainnet only; a Polygon extension (N$\approx$300) from our companion work will merge into v5. \emph{Category imbalance.} \texttt{defi\_hf\_trader} dominates the agent class (17\%) and its mining rule is itself a behavioral heuristic ($>100$ tx/week), so classifiers relying on raw frequency will look artificially strong at Level~2; Step~2 of the audit harness partially flags this. \emph{Single-class platforms at Level 5.} Every platform contributes only one class, conflating domain shift with concept failure; v5 will add mixed-class platforms (e.g., Aave borrowers) to enable a cleaner test. \emph{Label granularity.} The binary agent/human label does not distinguish LLM agents, MEV searchers, liquidation bots, and DeFi management agents; our companion Paper~0 finds LLM agents are behaviorally indistinguishable from DeFi management agents on this feature set (F1~=~0.53), so this benchmark is \emph{not} recommended for LLM-specific detection. \emph{Feature set.} The 23 features are a specific choice; graph-topological and cross-protocol features are absent, but the benchmark supports user-supplied features via the raw parquets.

\emph{Future directions:} (1)~v5 mixed-class platforms; (2)~cross-chain extension (Polygon, Base, Arbitrum) as a Level~6; (3)~adversarial stress tests with synthetic evasive-agent traces; (4)~an LLM-agent subset drawing from ElizaOS / Virtuals once density permits; (5)~rolling temporal splits to establish a retraining cadence.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

OnChainAgentID packages a 1{,}147-address provenance-verified Ethereum dataset, a five-level evaluation protocol with frozen splits, a \texttt{pip}-installable leakage-audit harness, and baseline results for four tabular models and a graph attention network. The benchmark's design responds to a structural gap in blockchain classification research: existing datasets do not publish machine-checkable mining rules, do not ship cross-platform splits, and do not bundle a leakage-auditing tool. We hope the combination of frozen splits, a datasheet, a leaderboard template, and a one-command audit harness lowers the activation energy for honest agent-detection evaluation. The five-level collapse we observe on our own baselines (0.84 $\to$ 0.80 $\to$ 0.67 $\to$ 0.76 $\to$ 0.40/0.74) is offered not as a method contribution but as a self-audit case study: an existence proof that without all five levels, high agent-detection AUCs cannot be distinguished from label or platform fingerprints. All artifacts are released under CC~BY~4.0 (data) and MIT (code), with a Zenodo DOI issued at camera-ready.

\section{Full Feature Definitions}
\label{app:features}

Let $T = \{t_1,\ldots,t_n\}$ be transaction timestamps, $G = \{g_1,\ldots,g_n\}$ gas prices, and $C = \{c_1,\ldots,c_n\}$ target contracts.

\paragraph{Temporal (7).}
(1)~\texttt{tx\_interval\_mean}, mean of consecutive inter-transaction intervals. (2)~\texttt{tx\_interval\_std}, their std. (3)~\texttt{tx\_interval\_skewness}, their skew. (4)~\texttt{active\_hour\_entropy}, Shannon entropy of tx bucketed into 24 UTC hours. (5)~\texttt{night\_activity\_ratio}, fraction of tx in UTC 0:00--6:00. (6)~\texttt{weekend\_ratio}, fraction on Sat/Sun. (7)~\texttt{burst\_frequency}, fraction of consecutive pairs with interval $<10$~s.

\paragraph{Gas (6).}
(8)~\texttt{gas\_price\_round\_number\_ratio}. (9)~\texttt{gas\_price\_trailing\_zeros\_mean}. (10)~\texttt{gas\_limit\_precision} (gas\_limit relative to gas\_used). (11)~\texttt{gas\_price\_cv}. (12)~\texttt{eip1559\_priority\_fee\_precision}. (13)~\texttt{gas\_price\_nonce\_correlation}.

\paragraph{Interaction (5).}
(14)~\texttt{unique\_contracts\_ratio}. (15)~\texttt{top\_contract\_concentration} (HHI). (16)~\texttt{method\_id\_diversity}. (17)~\texttt{contract\_to\_eoa\_ratio}. (18)~\texttt{sequential\_pattern\_score} (n-gram repetition).

\paragraph{Approval/security (5).}
(19)~\texttt{unlimited\_approve\_ratio}, fraction of \texttt{approve()} at MaxUint256. (20)~\texttt{approve\_revoke\_ratio}. (21)~\texttt{unverified\_contract\_approve\_ratio}. (22)~\texttt{multi\_protocol\_interaction\_count}. (23)~\texttt{flash\_loan\_usage}.
